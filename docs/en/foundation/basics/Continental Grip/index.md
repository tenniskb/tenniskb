# The Continental Grip — Your Nine-Shot Universal Key

Watch a 3.5 player during a points, and you'll see it happen constantly: they grip for the forehand, switch for the backhand, switch again for the volley, switch again for the serve. Four grip changes in ten seconds — and four chances to grab the wrong one.

Here's what I want you to know: **there's one grip that unlocks nine different tennis shots, and you never have to switch out of it.** No fumbling, no mid-point grip changes, no guessing. Once it's in your hand, you have nine options instead of one.

## The Nine Shots, One Grip

The Continental covers:

1. Volley (forehand and backhand)
2. Slice groundstroke (forehand and backhand)
3. Serve (flat, slice, or kick)
4. Overhead
5. Lob (defensive and offensive)
6. Drop shot
7. Half-volley
8. Return of serve (block and redirect)
9. Approach shot (slice)

Think of it like a master key. A regular key opens one door. A master key opens nine. The 3.5 player juggling four different grips is carrying four separate keys, hoping to grab the right one under pressure. You, with the Continental, are just walking around with the master key already in your hand.

And realistically, at your level, **the Continental should be your grip for about 70% of the shots you hit in a match.** The other 30% — your forehand groundstroke and forehand return — call for Semi-Western or Eastern instead. But Continental is your default. Everything else is the exception.

### Why This Grip Is Worth Building Your Game Around, Especially Past 50

There are four solid reasons this grip pays off, and they get more important as you get older:

- **No grip change at the net.** The Continental handles both your forehand and backhand volleys. You're not switching mid-exchange — you're staying put.
- **No wrist snap on the slice.** The open face naturally produces underspin. Your wrist doesn't have to do anything dramatic to get it.
- **No elbow torque on the serve.** The forearm rotation the Continental calls for (pronation) is a natural motion — nothing forced, nothing twisting against the joint.
- **No extra shoulder load on the volley.** Because the Continental keeps your volley compact — a short 6-to-12-inch punch rather than a full swing — your shoulder does a lot less work.

The four joints that tend to go first as we age are the wrist, the elbow, the shoulder, and the knee, roughly in that order. The Continental protects the first three just by being the grip you're already using. That's not a coincidence — it's the whole reason this grip has stuck around as long as it has.

## Finding the Grip: The Bevels and the Hammer Pickup

Before you can find the Continental reliably, you need to understand the handle itself. A racket handle isn't a smooth cylinder — it has eight flat sides, called bevels, numbered 1 through 8 starting from the top and moving clockwise (for a right-hander).

- **Bevel 1** is the top flat surface — usually where the manufacturer's logo sits.
- **Bevel 2** is the next surface clockwise from that.
- Bevels 3 through 8 continue around the handle.

**The entire definition of the Continental grip is this: the base knuckle of your index finger sits on bevel 2.** That's it. Everything else about the grip follows from that one placement.

### The Hammer Pickup

This is the simplest, most repeatable way to find it:

1. Hold the racket out in front of you, strings vertical, head up.
2. Lay your hand flat against the strings — palm touching, fingers together.
3. Slide your hand straight down to the grip without rotating it, then close your fingers.

That's it. Your base knuckle lands on bevel 2 automatically, and the V between your thumb and index finger ends up on the top edge, right where it should be. The whole idea mirrors how you'd naturally pick up a hammer — your hand just finds the right spot without you thinking about it.

**Your hands have less grip strength than they used to, and that's exactly why this drill matters.** It works because the motion is repetitive — your hand learns the path through repetition, not through conscious effort. Do ten hammer pickups a day for a week, and by the second week your hand starts finding bevel 2 on its own, without you having to check.

### Three Mistakes That Throw This Off

- **Rotating your hand while sliding it down** — you end up on bevel 3 or 4 instead of bevel 2. Fix: slide straight down, no twisting.
- **Closing your fingers before your hand reaches the grip** — you end up gripping too high, near bevel 1. Fix: close your fingers only once your hand has actually reached the handle.
- **Holding the racket vertically instead of out in front of you** — the bevels sit at odd angles and you can't feel them properly. Fix: hold the racket out in front, strings vertical.

## Checking the Grip: The V and the Four Contact Points

Once you've found bevel 2, there are two things worth double-checking: the V between your thumb and index finger, and four specific contact points where your hand meets the handle. Get both right, and the grip works. Get either wrong, and you end up with a hybrid grip that quietly loads your wrist without you realizing why your slice feels off.

### The V — Your Steering Wheel

Picture a clock face sitting on top of the handle. Straight up is 12 o'clock, which lines up with bevel 1 (the logo). **For the Continental, your V should point to about 11 o'clock** — just slightly left of straight up, sitting right on the edge between bevels 1 and 2.

This one detail is the fastest way to sanity-check your grip mid-match. If your V has drifted:

- **12 o'clock** — that's Eastern, not Continental.
- **1 o'clock** — that's Semi-Western.
- **2 o'clock** — that's Western.

Most 3.5 players who think they're playing Continental actually have their V sitting at 12 or 1 o'clock. They carry that grip to the net and can't figure out why their volleys keep sailing or catching the frame. If your volleys feel unreliable, check the V before you check anything else.

### The Four Contact Points

Your hand meets the grip in four specific places, and each one does a different job:

- **Heel pad** (base of the thumb) — the anchor. It rests on the back bevel, bevel 5.
- **Base knuckle of the index finger** — the pointer. It sits on bevel 2, same as always.
- **Thumb** — the stabilizer. It lies along the back bevel; it doesn't wrap around the handle.
- **Index finger** — the trigger. It lies along bevel 1, ready to add pressure the instant you make contact.

If your heel pad sits too far forward, your grip is off. If your thumb wraps fully around the handle instead of lying flat, you're gripping too tight. If your index finger curls in instead of lying along the bevel, you lose leverage right when you need it. All four need to be right at once — it's a package deal.

## The Pressure Scale: How Hard You Hold, and When

Here's a concept most players never think about, and it matters more the older you get: **grip pressure isn't constant through a shot. It changes across three distinct phases.**

- **Setup** (your ready position, split-step, anticipation): about 2-3 out of 10. Loose hand, light racket, ready to react to whatever comes.
- **Swing** (the swing itself, from load to the contact zone): about 3-4 out of 10. Still loose — the racket should feel light in your hand.
- **Contact** (the instant the ball hits the strings): 7-9 out of 10. Now you firm up. The racket needs to be stable right at impact.

The mistake I see constantly is players holding the racket at a constant 7 out of 10 the entire time — through the setup, through the swing, right up to contact. That grips tires your forearm out fast, and by the fifth game you're gripping the racket like it owes you money and your shots are getting worse, not better.

A useful way to feel the three phases: think **"baby bird"** at setup (soft hand, barely holding on), **"slingshot stone"** during the swing (loose, light, letting the racket do the work), and **"wrist lock"** at contact (firm, stable, the ball goes where you aim). Three images, three pressures, three phases — and your body picks up the sequence through the imagery faster than it would through counting numbers in your head mid-swing.

Your forearm muscles fatigue faster than they used to, and a grip that stays at 7/10 the whole time burns through roughly three times the energy of one that moves through the three phases properly. Get this rhythm down and you'll have real energy left for the third set, instead of gripping for dear life on every point.

One shot breaks the general pattern: the **drop shot** stays soft even at contact — 2-3 out of 10 the whole way through, like you're holding a feather rather than gripping a racket. It's the gentlest shot in your bag for a reason.

The serve, by contrast, is the firmest — contact pressure up around 9/10 — because the ball's moving faster there than anywhere else, and you need the racket rock-stable to control it.

## Where This Actually Shows Up: The Nine Shots

Every one of these nine shots uses the exact same grip — what changes is your swing path, footwork, and the cue you're thinking about, not the grip itself.

| Shot | What it feels like | The cue to remember |
|---|---|---|
| Forehand & backhand volley | Soft hand, tiny compact punch, firm wrist at contact | "Block, don't swing" |
| Forehand & backhand slice | Soft hand, loose carving motion, firm wrist at contact | "Carve the ball, don't hit it" |
| Serve | Soft hand, loose toss and drop, firm wrist plus pronation | "Slingshot, not snap" |
| Overhead | Soft hand, loose turn, firm wrist | "Put it down, don't smash it" |
| Defensive lob | Soft hand, loose arc | "Rainbow, not bullet" |
| Drop shot | Soft the whole way through, even at contact | "Soft hands, like holding a feather" |
| Slice approach | Soft hand, loose carve, firm wrist | "Deep slice, then to the net" |
| Return of serve | Soft hand, block rather than swing, firm wrist (6-8/10) | "Block, don't swing" |

Notice that eight of the nine shots start soft at setup — the drop shot is the only one that stays soft all the way through contact. Everything else builds from soft to firm. Once that rhythm is in your hands, all nine shots start to feel like variations on the same theme instead of nine separate things to remember.

## Six Drills to Build This Into Your Hands

**1. The Hammer Pickup — solo, 3 minutes a day.** Stand in front of a mirror. Do 20 hammer pickups: hold the racket out, lay your hand flat on the strings, slide straight down with no rotation, close your fingers. Check each time — knuckle on bevel 2, V on the top edge. Goal: 20 out of 20 clean.

**2. V at 11 O'Clock — solo, 2 minutes a day.** Hold the racket in front of you and just watch the V as you rotate the racket slightly through 11, 12, 1, and 2 o'clock, naming each grip as you pass it. Twenty reps, all ending back at 11 o'clock.

**3. The Four Contact Points — solo, 3 minutes a day.** Take the grip and run the checklist: heel pad on bevel 5, knuckle on bevel 2, thumb along the back (not wrapped), index finger along bevel 1. Goal: four out of four, every time.

**4. The Three-Phase Pressure — solo, 3 minutes a day.** From the ready position, run the sequence out loud if it helps: "soft hand" at setup, "slingshot stone" through the swing, "firm wrist" at contact. Twenty reps, aiming for at least 18 with the pressure genuinely changing between phases.

**5. Nine Shots in Nine Minutes — solo or with a partner.** One minute each on forehand volley, backhand volley, forehand slice, backhand slice, serve, overhead, drop shot, defensive lob, and slice approach. For each one, check that you're in Continental and running the three-phase pressure. Goal: nine for nine.

**6. Match Practice — solo or with a partner, 5 minutes.** Play out points and ask yourself before every single shot: which grip does this need? Volley, slice, serve, lob, overhead, drop shot, approach, or return — Continental. Forehand groundstroke — Semi-Western. Aim for getting it right on 9 out of 10 shots.

## Seven Mistakes I See Constantly

| Mistake | What it looks like | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| V at 12 o'clock | V sits on bevel 1 — that's Eastern, not Continental | Recheck against the "11 o'clock" cue |
| V at 1 o'clock | V sits on bevel 2 — that's Semi-Western | Recheck against the "11 o'clock" cue |
| Hand rotates during pickup | Sliding while twisting lands you on bevel 3 or higher | "Straight down, no rotation" |
| Thumb wrapped around the handle | Grip is too tight, loses feel | "Thumb along the back, not wrapped around" |
| Constant 7/10 pressure | Gripping the same tightness through the whole swing | Run the three-phase scale — soft, loose, firm |
| Switching grips at the net | Eastern for forehand volley, scrambling to Continental for backhand | "Continental, always" — it's the same grip both sides |
| Wrong grip on the slice | Semi-Western closes the face, ball flies long | Continental keeps the face open — that's what makes the slice work |

## The 50+ Reality Check

- **Wrist pain on the slice** usually traces back to a Western or Semi-Western grip forcing your wrist to snap through the shot. The Continental's naturally open face gives you underspin without asking your wrist to do anything extra.
- **Tennis elbow from serving** often comes from a wrist snap taking the load that should be going through forearm rotation instead. The Continental's pronation is the joint-friendly way to generate that motion.
- **Shoulder soreness from volleying** usually means you're taking a full swing instead of a compact punch. Continental at the net means 6 inches of movement, not a full stroke.
- **Grip fatigue by the third set** comes from holding constant pressure instead of running the three-phase scale. Soft at setup, loose through the swing, firm only at contact — that rhythm is what saves your forearm for the shots that matter late in a match.
- **Fumbling grip changes at the net** disappears entirely once Continental becomes your one grip for both volleys — there's simply nothing to switch.

The single biggest thing to take from all of this: the Continental isn't just "a grip." It's the one piece of equipment knowledge that protects four joints, covers nine shots, and lets you keep playing well past the point where a lot of players start switching to easier sports. Learn this grip once, properly, and you'll be drawing on it for the next twenty years of tennis.
