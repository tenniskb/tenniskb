# The 4 Doubles Return Patterns

I get asked this a lot: "Why do I keep losing return points in doubles, even when my return is decent?" And almost every time, the answer is the same. You're returning like it's singles.

Here's the thing you need to understand first: in singles, the return is a survival shot. You just have to get it back. In doubles, the return is a setup shot — and it has to deal with two threats at once, not one. There's the server coming at you, sure, but there's also a net player on the other side just waiting to pounce on anything that floats into the middle of the court. Hit your return the way you would in singles — flat and cross-court — and you're not beating the server. You're feeding the poacher. That single habit is Here's the reason a lot of solid singles players lose 80% of their return points when they switch to doubles.

So forget "just get it back." Every return in doubles should be one of four patterns, and each one is a complete plan — what shot to hit, what to do the instant after you hit it, and how you and your partner move together. think of it this way a chess opening. You've got a handful of good first moves, and each one leads somewhere different. Pick the one that fits the position in front of you, and you're already playing a smarter game than the guy across the net who's just swinging and hoping.

Two things change from singles right out of the gate: where you aim, and how hard you hit it. In singles you're going cross-court nine times out of ten, hard and flat. In doubles, you want the middle 60% of the time, soft and low, with some slice or gentle topspin to keep the ball down. You're not trying to win the point off the return — you're trying to set up the next shot, or at minimum survive without handing the poacher a gift.

The four patterns, in brief:

- Return + Lob — a high, deep lob over both opponents. This your default against a hard first serve, especially as we get older.
- Return + 1 — a low, controlled return down the middle that lets your partner poach. The smart pattern.
- Return + Approach — a deep slice, and both you and your partner move to net together. The next step up in aggression.
- Return + 2 — a hard topspin drive, both of you stay at the baseline. For the brave — and I mean that.

They're not interchangeable options you pick at random. Each one resets the point into a different kind of middle-game: the lob resets you both to the baseline, neutral. Return + 1 either wins outright on the poach or keeps the rally going from a net-pressure position. Return + Approach puts both teams at the net for a volley battle. Return + 2 turns it into a baseline slugfest. Read the situation, then pick the pattern that matches — the pattern *is* the strategy.

Quick decision tree, the way I'd run it in my head standing on the baseline: fast first serve with an aggressive net player? Lob. Medium second serve with your partner camped at net ready to pounce? Return + 1. Soft second serve landing short, with gas in the tank for both of you? Return + Approach. Weak second serve and you're both baseliners today? Return + 2.

## Return + Lob — Your Insurance Policy

This the most underused shot in recreational doubles, and it shouldn't be. Most 3.5 players see a hard first serve coming and panic — they try to drive it back, and it either goes into the net, sails long, or lands right on the poacher's racket. The lob solves all three problems at once. It takes the pace off, it clears the net player entirely, and it buys you time to recover. If you already know the defensive slice lob from groundstroke rallies, good news — nothing changes here. Same Continental grip, same high backswing, same deep aim into the corner.

Picture the net player as the opponent's most dangerous piece — the one that can end the point on anything short. The lob doesn't beat them. It takes them off the board. It forces them to retreat and hit an overhead instead of a crisp volley, and suddenly the next shot is a groundstroke, not a kill shot. You haven't won the point. But you've taken away their biggest weapon, and the point is back to neutral.

When to pull the trigger: read three things at once — the serve is fast, the net player is up close and ready to pounce, and the ball's landing deep enough that you can't attack it anyway. If all three line up, lob. If even one doesn't, think about a different pattern.

How to hit it: Continental grip, no exceptions — a semi-western grip on a lob sends the ball flying long. Open or semi-open stance, same as your slice lob. The backswing needs to go up high — I tell people to "scratch your back" with the racket on the way up, because that's exactly the motion, and it's what gives the ball its arc. Contact happens out in front, roughly chest height, swinging low to high with a little shoulder shrug at the top to add extra height. Aim deep — within three feet of the baseline, toward the corner. You'll know you hit it clean by the sound: a soft "phập," not a flat "bộp."

After the ball's in the air, don't just stand there admiring it. Side-shuffle straight back to the center of the baseline. You hit the return up near the service line — the center of the baseline is six or seven meters back, and that's home. Your partner should be doing the same thing if they were up at net; the lob buys both of you time to reset. Now you're both back, the opponent's net player is back too, and the point restarts from neutral. That's the value of the lob — you didn't win anything, but you didn't lose anything either, and the next ball is a coin flip.

I'll be honest with you about why this matters more the older you get: the lob is about the lowest-stress shot in tennis. No shoulder load — it's a small swing and gravity does the work. No back strain — open stance, no twisting through contact. No knee stress — no sprinting, no lunging. If you're lobbing 80% of first serves, you're not playing scared. You're playing smart, and you're saving your shoulder for another twenty years of tennis.

## Return + 1 — The Smart Pattern

If Return + Lob is your insurance policy, Return + 1 is your best offense with the least risk. It's the smartest pattern in doubles because you don't have to hit a great shot to win the point — you just have to hit a *hittable* one and let your partner do the rest.

Here's the mechanic: you hit a low, controlled return down the middle. Your partner, already camped near net, poaches the next ball. Either the poach ends it right there, or the rally continues from a position where your team already has the net advantage. Think of your return as a handoff, not a weapon. You're not trying to win the point on this shot — you're trying to put the ball where your partner can put it away. The return is the pass; the poach is the shot.

Four things need to line up for a good one. First, keep it low — clearing the net by only a foot or two, no more. Go higher than that and you're handing the poacher an uncomfortable ball at chest height, which most 3.5 poachers will actually miss more often than put away. Second, aim down the middle — between the server and the poacher, not at either of them. Third, land it deep enough — inside the service box, ideally mid-box, so the server can't just step in and swat a winner. Fourth, keep the pace medium. Too hard and you're feeding the server pace; too soft and you're floating it up for an easy put-away.

The single biggest mistake I see at this level is hitting the return too high. It feels safe — clearing the net by three feet or more feels like you've got margin. But all you've done is hand the poacher a ball at chest height where they have to make a hard decision on the fly. Keep it low, knee-to-waist height, and the poach becomes almost automatic.

Which grip you use depends on which side the ball comes to. On the backhand side, stick with Continental and slice it — same grip you already use for volleys and lobs, so there's nothing new to learn, and slice naturally stays low. On the forehand side, switch to semi-western or eastern and hit a low topspin drive instead, since that grip naturally produces the dip you want. If you're building this shot from scratch, start with the backhand slice version. It's the easiest one to own at this level, and you can add the forehand drive later as a next step.

As for where exactly "the middle" is — it's a three-foot window, not a pinpoint. Anywhere from the center service line to about a foot and a half either side works. Picture a triangle: the server at one corner, the poacher at another, you at the third. The middle of the box is roughly the centroid of that triangle — equidistant from everyone — and the player closest to it, your poaching partner, gets there first. Aim for the middle, and the geometry does the rest of the work for you. Go cross-court instead and you've just aimed the ball straight at the person trying to intercept it.

Your job doesn't end once you've hit the return. Call "poach!" or "switch!" before you even hit the ball so your partner knows it's safe to move. If the poach happens, recover to cover the alley — your partner takes the middle, you take the outside. And keep an eye on the lob over your partner's head; if the opponent tries to go over the poach, you're the backstop. The best returners in this pattern aren't thinking "how do I win this point" — they're thinking "where can my partner actually hit a volley." That shift in mindset alone will improve your Return + 1 more than any technical tweak.

## Return + Approach — The Net Conversion

This the aggressive, next-level pattern: after your return, both you and your partner move forward to the net together. You hit a deep slice approach — same shot as any slice approach off a regular groundstroke, just under return-of-serve pressure and with less time to set up — and your partner, already at net, is waiting to join you in a two-on-two volley battle.

It's also the most joint-stressing of the four, so at 50+ This a "next step," not your everyday default. Lead with the lob or Return + 1 first, and bring this out when the conditions are right.

Picture it as a knight's charge in chess — it starts from the back, sees a gap, and jumps forward to attack. There's real risk (the knight can be traded) but a successful charge often just wins the point outright.

You'll want all four of these true before you commit to it: the second serve is soft enough to attack, your return position lets you hit a deep slice from around the service line, your partner's up at net ready to intercept, and — importantly — your legs and shoulders actually feel good enough for the transition today. If even one of those is a no, hit something else instead.

The shot itself: Continental grip, open or semi-open stance, same as any slice approach. The backswing is medium-high — not the full "scratch your back" of a lob, since you've got less time reacting to a serve, but not a flat regular slice swing either. Contact out in front around chest height, aim deep into the corner within three feet of the baseline. Same clean "phập" sound you're listening for on the lob.

Then the transition begins, and This where the real risk lives, especially as we get older: side-shuffle two or three steps to rebalance, then a diagonal sprint — at about 70%, not 100% — into the net. Arrive low, knees bent, weight forward, and split-step the instant the opponent makes contact with their reply. Total transition time is around a second, a second and a half. The sprint is the highest-risk moment in this whole pattern for older bodies — if your body's telling you "not today," don't force the approach. Hit the lob instead. The lob's always available. The approach is optional, and it should stay optional based on how you feel, not your ego.

Once you're both at net, you're in a genuine two-on-two battle, and the same three volley options from any net battle apply: crosscourt volley as your default — safe, deep, takes away the angle — a body shot at whoever's in the middle, or the down-the-line angle, which I'd leave for 4.5-level players only. At this level, crosscourt is your bread and butter. Once both players are up there, think of the team as a single joint rather than two separate people — you rotate, cover, and adjust together in real time. The approach is the move that creates that joint; every volley decision after is just the joint responding.

Given the physical cost, treat this as an occasional weapon at 50+ — once or twice a set, not every single point. Your knees, back, and heart rate will thank you.

## Return + 2 — For the Brave

This the most aggressive of the four, and honestly, the one I'd use least at this level. You hit a hard topspin drive down the middle or cross-court, and both of you stay put at the baseline. The point turns into a straight-up groundstroke duel.

Why I call it "for the brave": a real drive puts genuine load on the shoulder — we're talking 70+ mph groundstrokes, not a controlled tap. The rally that follows plays like singles — long exchanges, more running, more wear on the joints. And more often than not, the advantage in that exchange goes to the team already sitting at net, not the two of you grinding it out from the baseline.

It works when the second serve is genuinely weak — slow, short, sitting up — and both you and your partner are baseline players by nature. It fails badly when the serve's only medium (you can't really attack it) or when your partner's actually up at net and should be getting support for a poach instead.

The numbers back up my caution here: at the 3.5 level this pattern works maybe 40% of the time. Past 50, that drops closer to 25%. The hard drive loads the shoulder, the baseline rally loads the knees and back, and the net player's poach often ends the point before the rally even gets going. My advice past 50: lead with the lob, and only reach for this one when you and your partner are both clearly baseliners and the second serve is genuinely soft.

## Five Drills to Build the Habit This Week

1. The decision-tree shadow drill (solo, 3 minutes a day). Stand on the baseline and have a friend call out one of the four patterns — "Lob!" "One!" "Approach!" "Two!" You take one step in the matching direction: back foot for lob, no movement for One (the poach happens without you moving), forward for approach, no movement for Two. Repeat 20 times, mixing them randomly. Thisn't about the shot yet — it's training the decision itself, because at this level the wrong pattern, not the wrong technique, is the number one mistake.

2. The lob live-ball drill (with a partner, 10 minutes). Have your partner serve at first-serve pace. Every single return has to be a high, deep lob clearing an imaginary net player a meter off the net, followed by a side-shuffle straight back to the center baseline. Goal: ten in a row landing within three feet of the baseline. Cue words: "scratch your back" on the backswing, "deep into the corner" on the aim.

3. The middle-return drill (with a partner, 10 minutes). Partner serves at second-serve pace. Every return goes down the middle of the service box, followed by calling "Poach!" and side-stepping to the alley to simulate supporting the poach. Goal: ten in a row landing in the middle third of the box with controlled pace. Cue: "geometric sweet spot" — the middle of the triangle.

4. The slice-and-sprint drill (with a partner, 10 minutes). Partner serves at soft second-serve pace. Hit a deep slice approach, then run the full transition — side-shuffle, 70% sprint, split-step. Goal: five full sequences. Cues: "scratch your back" on the backswing, "70% sprint" on the transition, "arrive low" at net.

5. The pattern-match rally (with a partner, 15 minutes). Play out real doubles points, but call your pattern out loud before every return — "Lob!" "One!" "Approach!" "Two!" After each point, talk about whether it actually fit the situation, or whether something else would've worked better. Goal: ten points, all four patterns used at least once. This the drill that actually wires the situation-to-pattern instinct into your body — after ten reps or so, "fast serve" starts to mean "lob" without you even thinking about it.

## The Seven Mistakes I See Most at 3.5

| Mistake | What it looks like | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| Cross-court return in doubles | Old singles habit — returning straight into the poacher | Middle-return drill; aim middle 60% of the time |
| Return + 1 too high | Clears the net by 3+ feet, poacher can't put it away | Keep it low — 1-2 feet over the net |
| Lob too short | Lands at the service line, opponent smashes it | Lob-and-recover drill; "deep into the corner" |
| Sprinting at 100% on the approach | Full sprint, gassed by the time you reach net | "70% sprint" cue — smooth, not fast |
| Wrong pattern for the situation | Attacking a first serve, lobbing a soft second serve | Decision-tree drill; read first, return second |
| No communication | Silent returner and partner, collisions or missed poaches | Call it out — "Poach!" "Mine!" "Yours!" — every point |
| Forgetting to recover | Standing at the service line after a lob | Side-shuffle to center baseline every time |

## A Word on Playing This Past 50

A few honest notes on where the wear and tear actually shows up, and what to do about each one. Hard drives on Return + 2 load the shoulder — lean on the lob or Return + 1 instead. The sprint on Return + Approach loads the knees and back — keep it to 70%, and skip it entirely on a day your body says no. Repeated approaches in one set will spike your heart rate — once or twice a set is plenty, not every point. If your grip's too tight on the slice for Return + 1, you're inviting tennis elbow — soften it to about a 3 or 4 out of 10, the shot doesn't need power. If you can't hear "Mine!" over the wind or a hearing aid, switch to hand signals — fist for mine, open hand for yours. And if one missed return has you thinking "I can't return in doubles" — it's one shot. If the pattern was the right call, the next one's fine. Reset and move on.

If I can leave you with one rule above all the others: the lob is your 50+ insurance policy. Fast first serve — lob. Aggressive net player — lob. Body just doesn't feel right today — lob. It buys time, takes the net player out of the equation, and gives you and your partner a real chance to reset. A player who lobs 80% of first serves and leans on Return + 1 for second serves is playing about as smart as doubles gets — and it's the same player who's still playing pain-free twenty years from now.

There's a nice parallel here to internal martial arts, if that's a lens that speaks to you: the lob is *Lã Kình* — yielding to the fast serve and redirecting it, rather than meeting force with force. Return + 1 is *Tương Hỗ* — mutual support, two bodies moving as one. Return + Approach is *Hợp Nhất* — unifying at the net into a single unit. And the recovery after every lob, that side-shuffle back to center, is *Hoàn Thủ* — returning to your center, your home position, before the next exchange begins.

Start this week with Drill 1 — the decision-tree shadow drill. It's the foundation everything else builds on. Add Drill 2 once that feels automatic. Give it four weeks and you'll be picking the right pattern without having to think about it.
