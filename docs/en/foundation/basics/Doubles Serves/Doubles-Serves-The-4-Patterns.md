# Doubles Serves: The 4 Patterns Every 3.5 Player Should Own

Here's the thing I want you to understand before anything else: in singles, your serve can be a weapon. At 200 km/h it's an ace, at 150 it's still a real threat. In doubles, that same fast serve almost never wins the point outright — because your opponent's net partner is standing right there, ready to poach. A "fast enough to ace" serve is also fast enough to sail into the net, go long, or land right in the poacher's strike zone.

So let's reframe it. In doubles, your serve isn't a weapon. It's the opening move — think of it this way a chess opening. Each of the four patterns below leads to a different middle game, and your job is to pick the one that matches the situation and coordinates with your partner at the net.

## Why Doubles Serving Is a Different Game

In singles you're trying to either ace your opponent or set up a groundstroke rally, so you serve at full power, aim anywhere in the box, and mix in topspin, slice, or kick as you please. In doubles, the target shifts. You're serving at about 70–80% power, aiming at a specific target — wide, T, or body — with slice as your default spin, because a low, dipping ball denies the poach. Miss badly in singles and you've lost a point. Miss badly in doubles and you've handed the other team an ace *and* a poach winner, because now You've got two of them working against your one serve.

The most common 3.5 mistake is treating a doubles serve like a singles serve: swing as hard as possible, aim center, hope for the best. The ball ends up too hot for the returner to control but not fast enough to be unreturnable — so it floats right into the net or straight at the poacher. The fix is simple to say and takes reps to own: 70–80% power, slice, aimed at a real target.

Here's a quick map of how the four patterns feed into what happens next:

- Serve Wide pulls the returner off the court, so your poacher covers the middle. This your 50+ default.
- Serve to the T jams the returner in the center, giving your poacher two sides to cover — This the poacher's dream ball.
- Serve to the Body takes away the returner's swing entirely, so your poacher covers the open court.
- Serve + Volley turns you into the poacher yourself — an aggressive option for when you're feeling good.

Pick the pattern that fits the moment. The pattern *is* the strategy.

### Why the Slice Is Your Doubles Default, Especially After 50

If you're playing past 50, lean into the slice hard, and here's why. It's a brushing motion rather than a flat collision, so your shoulder isn't absorbing the impact — your strings are. That alone makes it the joint-friendlier choice. The ball also dips down through the strike zone and keeps curving downward, landing near the returner's feet instead of their shoulders, which gives you a much bigger margin for error. Because it curves away from a right-handed returner, they have to reach across their body to get to it — a weaker return almost every time. And on top of all that, most 3.5 players simply make more slice serves than flat ones: 70% slice-in beats 50% flat-in every time.

Think of the flat serve as a weapon and the slice as a setup. In doubles, you want the setup. A player who serves 80% slice and 20% flat is going to land 70%+ of their first serves, consistently pull returners off the court, and consistently feed hittable balls to their poacher. Lead with the slice. Save the flat for the big points.

### The Mechanics Refresher

The physical mechanics of the doubles serve are identical to your singles serve — grip, trophy, throw, toss, rhythm, contact, all the same fundamentals. Four quick reminders before we get into strategy:

- Grip: Continental, always — the V sits on bevel #2, the same grip you use for volleys.
- Trophy position: elbow up, racket above your ear, palm on edge — not the flat "waiter's tray" position.
- The throw: pronation, not a wrist snap. Your forearm rotates about 180° from trophy to follow-through.
- The toss: higher than feels natural, slightly in front, and you're brushing the ball at the peak of its rise.

What's new here isn't the swing — it's the strategy. Where to aim, when to use which pattern, and how to read and coordinate with your poacher.

## Serve Wide: Your 3.5 Default

Serve Wide is your bread-and-butter shot and your best insurance policy after 50. You slice the ball into the returner's backhand corner, and three things happen at once: the returner gets pulled wide off the court and has to run it down, their return comes back defensive and floaty because they had no time to set up, and your poacher gets a fat, hittable ball sitting right in the middle of the court.

Don't think of this as an attempt to ace anyone. Think of it as a trap. You're not trying to win the point on the serve — you're pulling the returner out of position so your partner can finish it on the very next ball.

The sequence: aim for the backhand corner (the deuce corner if you're a righty serving to the ad court), slice the ball at 70–80% power by brushing the right side, and call "Mine!" or "Poach!" to your partner *before* you serve so they know what's coming.

For your aim, you're looking at roughly 2–3 feet inside the singles sideline and 2–3 feet inside the baseline — deep and wide enough that the returner has to run forward *and* sideways to get there. Don't overthink the precision; it's a one-foot window, not a pinpoint. And there's a reason you're going to the backhand specifically: most 3.5 returners are noticeably more comfortable on the forehand, so a wide slice to the backhand either forces a defensive backhand or a cross-court forehand that lands right in the middle — either way, your poacher wins.

On the technical side, picture the ball as a clock face looking straight down at it. If you're a righty serving to the ad court, your contact point is around 3 o'clock — the right side of the ball. Your racket brushes from about 7 o'clock up to 3 o'clock, a diagonal path across the right side that spins the ball to the left. To set that contact up, toss about a foot to the right of center so the ball meets your racket on its right side, then pronate through contact just like normal — you're just making contact on the side of the ball rather than the back of it. Thisn't a slow, tentative swing; the slice moves at the same pace as a flat serve, it just curves. Stay around 70–80% power — control matters more than raw speed here.

The coordination piece is what actually makes this shot work as a team play. Before you serve, call "Poach!" or "Mine!" so your partner knows the ball is heading wide and can get ready to cover the middle. The instant the returner makes contact, call "Now!" or "Go!" — that's the signal for your partner to move. After the point, if the poach lands, you're already in good position to stay back; if it doesn't, shift over and cover the alley. If hearing calls over wind or crowd noise is an issue — which it often is once you're past 50 — switch to hand signals: a fist behind the back means "I'm poaching," an open hand means "stay put."

Quick reference — Serve Wide

| | |
|---|---|
| When | First serve, when the returner's backhand is the weaker wing |
| Aim | Deuce corner, 2–3 ft inside both the sideline and the baseline |
| Spin | Slice — brush the right side of the ball |
| Power | 70–80% |
| Trajectory | Low, dipping, curves left |
| Calls | "Poach!" or fist signal before the serve; "Now!"/"Go!" as the returner contacts |
| Why it works | Pulls the returner wide, poacher covers the middle |

## Serve to the T: Your Safest Target

If Serve Wide is your bread and butter, the T serve is your safety net. It's down the middle, into the service line, and it forces the returner to hit *up* — right into your poacher's wheelhouse. Three things make it the safest option in your arsenal: it's the shortest path to the service line, so the returner gets less reaction time; the high arc needed to clear the net forces their return upward, straight to your poacher; and because the ball is coming at their body's center, they get jammed and have to make a split-second forehand-or-backhand decision, and either choice tends to be defensive.

Picture it like a ceiling press. The ball goes up to clear the net, the returner's return has to go up again because they're jammed, and your poacher is waiting right at the low point of that arc. The geometry does the work for you.

This your go-to when your poacher is set and ready at the net, when the returner loves hitting cross-court, when they're crowding the sideline expecting something wide, or honestly whenever you just need a high-percentage, safe first serve. All four of those situations point to the same answer: go T.

Your target is the center service line, about a foot inside it — a one-foot window, not a pinpoint, so don't get precious about hitting it exactly. Because the T is closer to the net than a wide serve, the ball needs a steeper arc to clear it, and that higher arc is exactly what forces the return up. That's a feature of this serve, not something to flatten out — a flat T serve is really just a body serve wearing a different hat.

You can hit the T serve flat or sliced, and both work. Flat T means full power, roughly 90%, hit through the back of the ball — higher risk, higher reward, and best saved for the big points like 5-4 in a breaker. Slice T is the everyday version: 70–80% power, brushing the right side for extra curve, lower risk with only a slightly smaller reward. Lead with the slice T as your default. The toss for a T serve moves — toss about a foot left of center so the ball meets the racket on its left side and the face closes over the top of the ball. Keep this in mind: your toss steers the serve: right sends it wide with slice, center sends it flat down the middle, left sends it up and curving. If you can't control your toss direction yet, that's the first thing to fix before worrying about anything else here.

Quick reference — Serve T

| | |
|---|---|
| When | Poacher ready, returner likes cross-court, returner crowding the sideline, or you need a safety serve |
| Aim | Center service line, about 1 ft inside it |
| Spin | Flat or slice (slice is your default) |
| Power | 70–80% slice, ~90% flat |
| Trajectory | High arc, deep |
| Toss | About 1 ft left of center |
| Why it works | Forces the return up, poacher hits down |

## Serve to the Body: Your Underused Weapon

Most players at this level only really know two serves — wide and T. The body serve is the one they're leaving on the table, and it's genuinely devastating once you've got it. Hit it right at the returner's torso and they simply can't extend their arms to swing. What comes back is a half-volley or a block, a slow floater with almost no pace, and the returner is stuck in a defensive crouch with zero chance to attack. Your poacher basically gets all the time in the world.

Think of it as a jamming shot. Their arms get pinned to their body, their swing shrinks to nothing, and the return comes back slow and loopy almost every single time. It's about as close as you'll get to a guaranteed poach in doubles.

Reach for this one when the returner is crowding the line — standing two or three feet from the sideline, clearly braced for a wide serve — or when they've got a big forehand or backhand and love to attack, or when your partner is at the net signaling they're ready for a put-away, or even when the wind is making wide serves unpredictable and a T serve feels too obvious. Any one of those is reason enough; when several stack up together, the body serve is your clear answer. Here's the mechanism worth knowing: most 3.5 returners plant themselves two to three feet off the sideline, already braced for wide. The body serve catches them completely off guard — no swing, no attack, just a slow float that your poacher eats alive.

You're aiming one to two feet inside the center service line — the mirror image of the T target, but on the returner's body side — landing around belly-button or chest height. That's a one-to-two-foot window, and the goal is for the ball to actually hit the returner, not sail past them. This also where the risk lives: if the ball passes the returner instead of hitting them, it's just a fault, because the margin here is tighter than either the wide or the T serve. Save this one until your wide and T serves are already automatic — trying to force it in before then usually just means extra faults.

Quick reference — Serve Body

| | |
|---|---|
| When | Returner crowds the line, likes to attack, poacher's ready to finish, or wind makes wide serves risky |
| Aim | 1–2 ft inside the center line, at the returner's torso |
| Spin | Flat, or slice |
| Power | ~80% |
| Trajectory | Flat, fast, straight at the body |
| Toss | About 1 ft right of center |
| Why it works | Jams the returner — no swing, no attack, slow floater |
| Watch for | If the returner's well off the body line, skip this one and go safe instead |

## Serve + Volley: For the Brave

This the most aggressive of the four patterns, and it's the one where you become both the server *and* the poacher — no partner needed to finish the point, because you're following your own serve straight to the net. It shines when you don't have a partner to poach for you, when the returner's return is genuinely weak, or when your body just wants a change of pace mid-match.

The sequence is simple: serve (usually your wide slice, since it's your most reliable default), split-step the instant the returner makes contact, then move to the net at around 70% sprint speed — not a full-out charge, your knees will thank you — arrive, split-step again, and volley.

If you're past 50, treat this one carefully. The serve itself is joint-friendly — Continental grip, slice, 70% power, low load throughout. The real cost is the sprint to the net, so use this pattern once or twice a set, and only on points where your body actually feels good that day. Your heart, knees, and back will all notice the difference.

Quick reference — Serve + Volley

| | |
|---|---|
| When | No partner, weak returner, or you want a change of pace |
| Serve | Wide slice (your default) |
| Transition | Split-step → 70% sprint → split-step → volley |
| Net finish | Crosscourt volley by default |
| Frequency | Once or twice per set — not every point |
| Skip it if | Your body's telling you no sprints today, or the returner's a strong attacker |

## Five Drills to Build This Week

1. Aim Only (solo, 5 min/day). Stand at the baseline and set three cones or towels as targets: one in the wide deuce corner, one on the center service line about a foot in, and one 1–2 feet inside the center line at body height. Hit ten serves at each cone. You're aiming for 7 out of 10 landing in the window.

2. Slice vs. Flat (solo, 5 min/day). Hit ten flat serves at the wide corner, then ten slice serves at the same target. Pay attention to the difference — the flat one goes straight and fast and then just drops, with little margin, while the slice curves and dips and lands near the returner's feet with a lot more room for error. This the drill that makes "slice is the smarter default" click for real.

3. Call the Poach (with a partner, 10 min). You serve while your partner stands at the net. Before every serve, they flash a hand signal — fist for "I'm poaching," open hand for "stay," or a point to the alley for "cover the alley." Your job is to read the signal and serve to the target it calls for. Goal: 20 serves with 18 correct reads.

4. The Four Patterns Rotation (with a partner, 15 min). Play a practice set where your partner calls out one of the four patterns — Wide, T, Body, or +Volley — before each service game, and you execute exactly that. After each game, talk it through: did the pattern do its job? Did the poach actually happen? What would you change? Aim for four games, one of each pattern.

5. Toss Direction (solo, 3 min/day). Stand at the baseline and toss ten balls, cycling through three positions — a foot right of center for slice wide, dead center for a flat T, and a foot left of center for a kick or slice T. Don't even swing, just toss and let the ball bounce. You're checking whether your toss lands in the right zone 30 out of 30 times.

## Cues Worth Remembering

Slice is your default — you're looking at roughly 80% slice, 20% flat or kick, on every serve. Your toss is the steering wheel: it controls direction before you've even swung, so pick your target and aim your toss before you commit to the swing. Stay at 70–80% power; a smart doubles serve is never a max-effort swing. Use a hand signal before every serve so a missed verbal call never costs you a point, and call "Now!" or "Go!" the instant the returner makes contact. Keep the same serve under pressure — don't get cute at 5-4 in the breaker. Grip stays Continental, always. Trophy position is elbow up, racket above your ear, palm on edge — never a flat waiter's tray. The throw is pronation, not a wrist snap. If you're doing Serve + Volley, sprint at 70%, not 100% — save your knees. And above everything: survive first, place the ball second. Getting the serve in matters more than where exactly it lands.

## The Seven Mistakes Almost Everyone Makes at 3.5

| Mistake | What it looks like | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| Blasting the serve | Full power on every serve, ball in the net or long | Drop to 70–80% power, add slice and real placement |
| No specific target | Aiming "somewhere in the box" | Pick a one-foot window — a cone, not a vague zone |
| Wrong toss direction | Tossing center when you meant to go wide | Remember: toss is the steering wheel — toss right for wide |
| The waiter's tray | Palm flat and forward in the trophy position | Cue yourself "palm on edge," V to bevel #2 |
| Wrist snap instead of pronation | Snapping the wrist at contact, elbow starts to ache | Focus on pronation — forearm rotates, not the wrist |
| No coordination with your poacher | Serving without telling your partner where it's going | Hand signal before, "Now!" call at contact |
| Serving into the alley | Wide serve sails out for a double fault | Aim 2–3 ft *inside* the sideline, not right on the line |

## A Reality Check for Players Past 50

Your body has real, specific vulnerabilities that these patterns either protect or expose, so it's worth being honest about each one. A flat serve loads the rotator cuff, so slice becomes your default precisely because it asks less of the shoulder. A kick serve stresses the medial elbow, so save it for days your arm actually feels good and lead with slice the rest of the time. The sprint on Serve + Volley can push your heart rate past 180, so keep that pattern to once or twice a set rather than every point, and when you do sprint, go at 70% — smooth, not fast, to protect your knees. If your hearing makes it hard to catch a shouted "Poach!" over wind or crowd noise, hand signals before every serve solve that cleanly. And the kick serve's back arch is worth skipping on days your lower back isn't cooperating.

One missed serve doesn't mean you need to start lobbing everything in out of fear — that's the trap. Trust the same serve under pressure, and remember that 70% power *is* the smart power, not a compromise.

If there's one rule to take from all of this, it's that the slice serve is your insurance policy. A player who serves 80% slice and 20% flat or kick, places the ball with intent, and coordinates with their poacher is going to win roughly half their service games — and more importantly, they're going to still be playing in ten and twenty years, because their rotator cuff isn't taking a beating on every single point. Lead with the slice. Treat flat and kick as options for special occasions, not as your baseline. The body that doesn't load the shoulder on every serve is the body that keeps playing.

The serve in doubles isn't about power — it's placement, it's coordination, and honestly, it's longevity. Four patterns, four different setups for four different situations. If you're playing past 50, your default answer for 80% of your serves is the slice wide to the backhand corner. That's not power talking. That's placement, that's teamwork, and that's what keeps you on the court for years to come.

Start this week with Drill 1 — aim only, five minutes a day. That's the foundation everything else builds on. Then bring in Drill 3, calling the poach with your partner, to start locking in the coordination. Give it four weeks and you'll be picking the right pattern for the situation without even thinking about it.
