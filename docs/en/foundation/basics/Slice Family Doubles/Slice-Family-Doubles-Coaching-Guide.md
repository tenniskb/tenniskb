# The Slice Family in Doubles: An 8-Shot Playbook

*A coaching guide for the 3.5 recreational doubles team that wants every slice to work for two players, not just one.*

---

## Chapter 1 — Every Slice Is a Team Shot

In singles, the slice family gives you seven variations for one player. In doubles, it's eight variations for two players — the eighth being the slice wide serve, the one you actually serve with. But the real math doesn't stop there. Every one of those variations picks up a doubles angle, because your partner is now a third variable in every decision.

Take the slice return. In singles, it's simple: block and redirect, deep down the middle. In doubles, it's the same swing — but now you've got a partner to set up at the net, and an opponent's partner at the net you need to stay away from. The doubles slice is the singles slice plus partner coordination. Every single slice you hit carries a doubles angle, a role for your partner, and some amount of poach risk.

Think of it as a chess game with four pieces on the board — you, your partner, the server, and the server's partner. Every slice you hit is a move that touches all four. A slice return to the deep middle isn't just a return — it's a setup for your partner's poach, a denial of the opponent's angle, and a positioning cue for the server, all in the same shot. One shot, three jobs.

Here's the full picture of how each variation shifts once a partner enters the frame:

| # | Variation | Doubles angle | Who hits it | Partner's role | Poach risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Slice Wide Serve | Pulls the returner wide, sets up the poach | Server | Poach (the 50% rule) | High — poaching is the plan |
| 1 | Slice Return | Deep middle, sets up the partner | Returner | Poach if the ball sits up | Medium — avoid the net player |
| 2 | Slice Low Ball | Defensive baseline rally | Either player | Stay at net, don't poach | Low — safe to dig out |
| 3 | Slice Volley | At the net, finishes the point | Net player | Cover the alley | N/A — you are the poach |
| 4 | Slice Drop Shot | At the net, the change-up | Net player | Cover the alley, expect a dig | N/A |
| 5 | Slice Defensive Lob | Resets the point when the opponent's at net | Either player | Move back to the baseline | N/A — the lob is the escape |
| 6 | Slice Counter-Punch | Stretched wide, neutral | Either player | Cover the open court | Low — This a defensive shot |
| 7 | Slice Approach | Return-plus-approach pattern | Returner | Stay at net, don't poach | Medium — your slice is the setup |

Most 3.5 doubles players run singles patterns straight into doubles — they hit the slice return like they're playing alone, with no awareness of their partner and no awareness of the opponent's net player. The doubles slice is genuinely a different shot from the singles slice, because the partner changes everything about it.

A few things worth holding in your head at all times: the slice is almost never the shot that wins the point outright — it's the setup for your partner's put-away, or for the poach, or for the lob. Whenever the server slices and the poacher volleys, that's one team play, not two separate shots. Whenever your net player poaches or volleys, the other player covers the alley automatically. And every serve should carry a hand signal before it happens — a fist for poach, an open hand for stay — because at 50+ you can't always count on hearing a shouted call over the wind or your own hearing aids.

The 3.5 player who thinks the slice is the winner loses more points than the player who understands the slice is the setup. The slice is the opening move in chess. The next shot is the middle game. The point gets won in the middle game.

## Chapter 2 — The Server's Slice: Wide Serve and Return

The server is the first actor in a doubles point, and they've got two slice variations to work with in the opening: the slice wide serve, and — on the next return of serve — the slice return.

The slice wide serve is your default first serve in doubles, and it should be. Slice it to the backhand corner, pull the returner wide off the court, and set up your partner's poach. Three reasons this earns the top spot: it forces the returner to run, which turns their return into a defensive, floating shot back toward the middle. It sets up a high-percentage poach for your partner at the net, and the poach is what actually wins the point. And it separates the two threats you're facing — the returner and the opposing net player. Pull the returner wide and they're on the outside, the exact opposite side from where the opposing net player would coordinate. A doubles point gets won by keeping those two threats separated; when they overlap in the middle, they can support each other, and when they're split apart, they can't.

Coordinating this with your partner is straightforward once you've drilled it: before the serve, a fist means "I'm poaching," an open hand means "I'm staying." You aim for the deuce corner and hit the slice at 70-80% power, giving it one or two bounces of margin and a body check on your swing. As the returner makes contact, call "now" or "go" — your partner poaches to the middle while you stay back and watch, ready to cover the alley if the poach connects. At 50+, you may genuinely not hear your partner's call over ambient noise or your own hearing — that's exactly why the pre-serve hand signal exists. It's silent, visible, and completely reliable regardless of hearing. Every doubles serve deserves a hand signal before the toss.

The slice return works differently in doubles than it does in singles. In singles, the deep middle is simply a safe target. In doubles, it's a setup for your partner's poach. You've actually got three doubles-specific targets to choose from: deep middle to set up your partner if they're poaching; at the server's feet if they're following their serve in (jamming a serve-and-volleyer is genuinely effective); or cross-court, away from the net player, if the opposing net player is the one poaching. In singles the slice return targets a corner. In doubles it targets a person — your partner or the situation, not a spot on the court. The return isn't really a shot in its own right; it's a setup for whatever comes next.

Here's how to read the situation and pick your target:

| Situation | Target | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Your partner is poaching (fist signal) | Deep middle — their wheelhouse | Sets up the poach |
| The opponent is poaching (you can see them at net) | Cross-court, away from the poach | Denies the poach |
| The server is following their serve to the net | At the server's feet | Jams the volley |
| No signal, nothing to read | Deep middle (the 50+ default) | Safe and sets up the next shot |

| | Slice Wide Serve | Slice Return |
|---|---|---|
| Who hits it | Server, the opening move | Returner, the response |
| Grip | Continental | Continental |
| Aim | Deuce corner, 2-3 ft inside the line | Depends — deep middle, cross-court, or feet |
| Power | 70-80% of full | 6-inch punch, no swing |
| Spin | Slice, the 50+ default | Slice — block and redirect |
| Partner's role | Poach, following the 50% rule | Poach or cover, depending on the read |
| Why | Separates the returner from the net player | Sets up the next shot |
| 50+ frequency | About 60% of first serves | 5-10 times per match |

## Chapter 3 — The Net Player's Slice: Approach and Volley

The net player is the second actor in the sequence — the one who actually finishes what the server's slice set up. Two variations live here: the slice approach, which is how you get to the net in the first place, and the slice volley, the compact shot that finishes the point once you've arrived.

The slice approach in doubles is usually the "return-plus-approach" pattern — slice the return deep, follow it to the net at 70% pace, and finish with a volley. Four steps make up the whole thing: decide (is the ball short, is your opponent off-balance, can you actually hit a deep slice, is the open court visible), slice (Continental grip, deep to the corner, underspin, 70% power), transition (split-step, sprint at 70% — never 100% — then split-step again at the net), and volley (Continental, L-shape wrist, a 6-inch punch, cross-court by default or down the line if the situation calls for it).

In doubles this whole sequence involves four actors working in order: you decide, you slice, the returner hits back, then you and your partner finish it together at the net. The approach was never one shot — it's a four-act play, where the slice sets the stage and the volley wins it.

Run through the same four questions every time before you commit:

| Question | If the answer is no |
|---|---|
| Is the ball short? | Stay at the baseline, hit another groundstroke |
| Is the opponent off-balance? | Wait for a better ball |
| Can you hit a deep slice? | Approach with topspin instead, if you're feeling bold |
| Is the open court visible? | Hit a cross-court approach instead — it's safer |

And past 50, the sprint itself deserves real caution: a full sprint to the net loads the knees, the back, and the heart in a way that adds up over a match. A 70% sprint that arrives 0.3 seconds later gets you to the same place — minus the wear. Smooth, not fast, saves the knees.

The slice volley is the most common slice variation you'll hit in doubles, full stop — somewhere between 30 and 50 per match. After the slice wide serve pulls the returner off the court, it's the poacher's slice volley that actually finishes the point. You've got five options here: cross-court, which is your default about 60% of the time, going away from a returner who's already wide; down the line, maybe 20% of the time, as a surprise when they're expecting cross-court; a drop volley, roughly 10% of the time, a soft slice that dies at the net; a half-volley, around 5%, for a ball that's already bounced below net height; and a lob volley, another 5%, hit high over an opponent's reach when they've crashed the net themselves.

After a slice wide serve, the returner is out wide — so the cross-court volley goes away from them, and they have to run back across the whole court just to reach it. That single choice takes a poach win rate from around 30% up to roughly 60%, simply because the returner's recovery is always slower than the volley's arrival.

| Situation | Best volley option |
|---|---|
| Returner is wide, from a slice wide serve | Cross-court — the default |
| Returner is recovering, moving back to center | Down the line — the surprise |
| Returner has come to net themselves | Lob volley, over their head |
| Returner is deep and you can see the angle | Drop volley, over the net |
| Ball is below net height | Half-volley — block and redirect |

| | Slice Approach | Slice Volley |
|---|---|---|
| Who hits it | Returner, becoming the net player | Net player, already there |
| Grip | Continental | Continental |
| Aim | Deep to the corner | Cross-court, 60% default |
| Power | 70% of full (slower than topspin) | 50-60 mph, compact |
| Spin | Slice, underspin | Slice, open face, downward path |
| Partner's role | Stay at net, don't poach yet | Cover the alley |
| Why | Brings you to the net | Finishes the point |
| 50+ frequency | 1-2 per set | 30-50 per match |

## Chapter 4 — The Change-Up Slice: Drop Shot, Defensive Lob, Counter-Punch

These three variations exist to break the pattern. Once a returner expects the slice wide, expects the poach, expects the lob, the change-up slice becomes the surprise that keeps them honest.

The slice drop shot is the net player's surprise weapon. You're at the net, the returner's stuck at the baseline, and you hit a soft, short slice that dies right at the net. Three situations make it the right call: you're at net and your opponent's deep with a visible angle in front of you; you're at net and they're at the baseline in a lazy, flat-footed stance; or you're at net right after they've just hit a defensive lob and are still scrambling back. In doubles you actually get more drop-shot chances than you would in singles, because the returner is often stuck deep covering the lob threat while your own partner holds the net — and a drop shot landing in the service box wins the point roughly 80% of the time at this level, simply because the returner starts too far away to reach it in time.

The team choreography is simple: you aim for the service box with barely any grip pressure — think 2 out of 10 — while your partner covers the alley in case the opponent manages to dig it out, and watches for the follow-up.

The slice defensive lob is the "return-plus-lob" pattern, and it's the 50+ default in doubles for good reason. Lobbed deep into the corner, over both opponents, it resets the point entirely. It clears both threats at once — the server and their net partner — buying you three to five seconds while they scramble from net back to baseline. It also resets your team's positioning: after the lob, you and your partner are both back at the baseline, ready for whatever comes next. And critically, it asks nothing of your body — high arc, low racket speed, no sprinting involved. This a genuinely joint-friendly shot.

A 50+ doubles player who lobs 80% of their returns against a crowding net player is a player who's still going to be on court in twenty years. The lob was never required to be a winner — it just needs to clear the net player and land deep. A body that isn't sprinting to net on every single return is a body still playing doubles at 60.

The slice counter-punch is your survival shot when you're stretched wide with a net player lurking. In doubles it's genuinely different from the singles version, because there's now someone at net ready to pounce on anything short. The counter-punch has to go to the deep corner, away from that net player. Three targets work here: the deep corner away from the net player, which is the longest distance and denies the poach outright; a lob over the net player if you're really stretched and out of better options; or straight at the net player's feet if they're closing in fast, which jams their reaction. Think of it as a two-in-one shot — escape plus recovery. You escape the stretched position by sending the ball deep, and you recover by trusting your partner to cover the alley while you scramble back to a ready stance. A 3.5 player who tries to actually win the point from a stretched counter-punch loses about 70% of the time. This shot's job is survival, not victory.

| | Slice Drop Shot | Slice Defensive Lob | Slice Counter-Punch |
|---|---|---|---|
| When | At net, opponent deep | Opponent at net, under pressure | Stretched wide, opponent at net |
| Grip | Continental | Continental | Continental |
| Power | 20-30 mph, soft | 50-60 mph, controlled | 30-40 mph, a block |
| Target | Service box, dies at the net | Deep corner, over the net player | Deep corner, away from the net player |
| Spin | Slice, underspin | Slice, a rainbow arc | Slice, block |
| Partner's role | Cover the alley | Move to the baseline | Cover the alley |
| Why | Surprise change-up | Buys time, resets the point | Escape, then recover |
| 50+ frequency | 1-2 per set | Often — the 50+ default | Sometimes, as an emergency |

## Chapter 5 — The Team Slice: Coordination, Drills, and the 50+ Reality Check

Four cues carry the coordination side of this whole system. The slice is a setup, not the winner — remember that on every single slice you hit. Server-plus-poacher means the server slices and the poacher volleys as one combined team play. Cover the alley means whenever your net player poaches or volleys, the other player automatically slides over to cover the wide shot. And talk with hands means a pre-serve hand signal every time — fist for poach, open hand for stay, a point toward the alley for "cover the wide shot."

The four hand signals themselves are worth drilling until they're automatic: a closed fist behind the back means "I'm poaching." An open hand, palm back, means "stay — don't poach, hold position." A finger pointed at the alley means "cover the alley — I'm going middle, you take the wide shot." And a hand tapped on the chest means simply "me — I've got this ball, you stay back." At 50+, you may genuinely not hear a partner's shouted call, and hand signals solve that completely — they're silent, visible, and work regardless of hearing. Before every serve, the net player shows a signal. Before every return, the partner shows one too. These signals are the actual language of doubles once you're past 50.

Here's the full decision map across all eight variations, useful to internalize as a single reference:

| Your situation | First choice | Backup option | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| You're serving, opening the point | Slice Wide Serve (60%) | Slice T (30%), body (10%) | Sets up the poach |
| You're returning a big serve | Slice Return, deep middle | At server's feet, or cross-court | Sets up the next shot |
| You're returning a weak second serve | Slice Approach | Topspin drive, if you're feeling bold | Brings you to the net |
| You're at net, normal pace incoming | Slice Volley, cross-court | Down the line, or a drop volley | Finishes the point |
| You're at net, opponent's at the baseline | Slice Drop Shot | Slice volley, if they're close | Surprise change-up |
| You're at baseline, opponent's at net | Slice Defensive Lob | Slice approach, if you have the choice | Buys time, resets |
| You're stretched wide, no time | Slice Counter-Punch, cross-court | Lob, if you're very stretched | Escapes and recovers |
| You're at baseline with a low skidding ball | Slice Low Ball | Slice volley, if you're already at net | Defensive survival |

Five drills for this week.

*Hand signal practice* (with your partner, 3 minutes a day). Run through all four signals — fist, open hand, point to the alley, hand on chest — with both partners calling out the meaning aloud to confirm Hiểu. Aim for 20 signals with all 20 correctly read.

*Slice wide plus poach* (with your partner, 10 minutes). You serve wide with slice, your partner's at net and shows a signal before you serve — fist means they poach, open hand means they stay, a point to the alley means they poach while you cover the wide shot. Aim for 20 serves with 18 correctly coordinated.

*Slice return plus partner* (with your partner, 10 minutes). Your partner serves wide with slice, you return with your own slice while your partner at net shows a signal for what happens next — fist to poach the return, open hand to stay, chest tap for "I've got this one." Return to the deep middle and let your partner's signal dictate their movement. Aim for 20 returns with 18 correctly coordinated.

*Slice approach in doubles* (with your partner, 15 minutes). Play a practice set. Every time you get a short ball, run the decision: approach or stay? If you approach, hit the slice, follow at 70%, finish with the volley — your partner holds the net position while you approach and covers the alley once you volley. Aim for one approach per game, with all four decision questions answered yes beforehand.

*Full playbook match practice* (solo or with a partner, 10 minutes). Play a practice set and try to use all eight slice variations at least once — wide serve, return, low ball, approach, volley, drop shot, defensive lob, counter-punch. After the set, count which ones you actually used and which you missed. The goal is 8 for 8 in a single set.

Cues worth keeping in your head all match:

- "Continental, always" — V to bevel #2, on every single slice.
- "Slice is a setup" — it's rarely the winning shot itself.
- "Server plus poacher" — one combined team play, not two separate shots.
- "Cover the alley" — the off-player covers wide the instant the other poaches or volleys.
- "Talk with hands" — a signal before every serve and every return.
- "Slice wide plus poach" — wide serve, then poach the middle, 60% of first serves.
- "Slice return to the deep middle" — block it and set your partner up.
- "Slice approach at 70%" — smooth, not fast, on the sprint.
- "Slice volley cross-court" — the doubles default, 60% of the time.
- "Slice drop shot at net" — soft, dies at the net, when they're deep.
- "Slice defensive lob over both" — high arc, deep corner, clears both opponents.
- "Slice counter-punch cross-court" — deep corner, away from the net player.
- "70% on the slice" — every variation, every time.
- "The lob is your 50+ insurance" — the defensive lob is your default under pressure.

Seven errors worth watching for:

| Error | What it looks like | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| No hand signal | Serving without telling your partner the poach plan | Talk with hands — fist, open hand, point |
| Targeting the wrong place | Returning to a corner like it's singles, instead of to your partner | Target the partner, not the corner — deep middle for the poach |
| Forgetting the alley | Net player poaches, off-player doesn't slide over | Cover the alley — the off-player takes the wide shot every time |
| Swinging at the wide slice | Trying to "hit" a wide slice and netting it or sending it long | Slice is a setup — placement, not power |
| Sprinting at 100% | Full sprint to the net, knees and heart paying for it | Sprint at 70% — smooth, not fast |
| Lobbing to the net player | Lob lands within the net player's reach | Lob over both — high arc, deep corner |
| Trying to win the counter-punch | Attacking from a stretched, off-balance position | Escape and recover — deep corner, then reset |

The 50+ reality check. A flat serve loads the rotator cuff more than you'd think, so the slice wide serve stays your 50+ default with meaningfully less shoulder load. A topspin return puts more strain on the medial elbow, so the slice return is your 50+ default there too. A full sprint to the net loads the knees directly, so run the slice approach at 70%. A poach can spike your heart rate past 180, so the pre-serve hand signal matters — only poach when you're actually ready, not on reflex. If you can't hear a shouted "poach" over hearing loss or hearing aids, pre-serve hand signals solve that completely, since they're silent and visible regardless. Reach on wide balls shortens somewhat with age, and the slice counter-punch compensates by letting the 27-inch racket do the reaching for you. And fine motor control on the drop shot holds up better with soft hands and a light 2-out-of-10 grip pressure rather than tension.

If there's one principle worth holding onto above all the rest: the slice is genuinely the 50+ doubles system. Eight variations across two players adds up to a sixteen-shot playbook, and the 50+ player who actually uses it plays doubles for twenty-plus more years. The 50+ player who runs singles patterns in doubles instead is looking at maybe ten. The Continental grip is the foundation everything else sits on. The hand signal is the communication layer. The slice is the shot itself. A body that draws on all eight variations is a body that stays on the doubles court for the next two decades.

If the tai chi framing helps you hold this together: the server's slice plus the poacher's volley is *Tương Hỗ* — mutual support, two players functioning as one team. The hand signal is *Hợp Nhất* — unity, silent coordination that makes the team feel like a single mind. The Continental grip itself is *Tùng* — soft, adaptive, carrying no tension. The slice defensive lob is *Lã Kình* — yielding to the net player and redirecting the energy up and over them rather than fighting through it. The slice volley is *An Thủ* — pressing with the racket, no swing at all. The slice counter-punch is *Hoàn Thủ* — returning to a ready position after being stretched. And the 70% sprint is *Phóng Tùng* — a full-body release, smooth rather than forced.

## Match-Day Mental Checklist

```
BEFORE EVERY SERVE (server):
 - Hand signal from net player (fist = poach, open = stay, point = cover alley)
 - Aim: Slice Wide (60%), Slice T (30%), Slice Body (10%)
 - Power: 70-80%, slice is the default
 - Call "Now!" or "Go!" as the returner makes contact

BEFORE EVERY RETURN (returner):
 - Hand signal from partner (fist = poach, open = stay)
 - Aim: deep middle (50% default), cross-court (deny the poach), or at the server's feet
 - Block, don't swing — 6-inch punch, no swing
 - Remember: slice is a setup, not a winner

AT THE NET (net player):
 - Slice Volley: cross-court (60% default), down the line (20%), drop (10%)
 - Open face, downward path, 6-inch punch
 - Cover the alley when the off-player poaches
 - Slice Drop Shot when the opponent is deep

UNDER PRESSURE (opponent at net):
 - Slice Defensive Lob: high arc, deep corner, over BOTH opponents
 - The 50+ default: the lob is your insurance policy
 - Buy time, reset, recover to the baseline

STRETCHED WIDE:
 - Slice Counter-Punch: cross-court, away from the net player
 - Reach with the racket, not the body
 - Escape and recover — don't try to win the counter-punch
```
