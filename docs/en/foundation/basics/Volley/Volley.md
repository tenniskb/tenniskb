# The Volley — The Art of Redirection

Watch Martina Navratilova at the net. She barely moves her arm. The ball comes in, her racket face tilts a few degrees, the ball goes back. The whole exchange looks like a conversation, not a fight.

Recreational players do the opposite. They muscle the volley like a groundstroke, swing hard, and the ball flies long or into the net. The whole point of coming to the net — pressure, angles, quick points — gets lost in that swing.

Here's the secret: it's not more effort, it's different effort. You're not generating pace — the incoming ball already has plenty. You're redirecting that pace. A volley is closer to catching a ball and gently tossing it sideways than to swinging a bat. In tai chi This called *Lã Kình* (捋勁), rollback energy — you yield to incoming force and subtly divert it. Same principle here. The ball brings the power. You bring the angle.

Picture a billiard ball hitting the cushion. The cushion doesn't move. The ball redirects. Your racket face is the cushion — the angle of the face is the angle of the rebound. That's it. No swing required.

Most 3.5 players, especially over 50, are afraid at the net. The ball comes fast, and the instinct is to step back, grip tight, and swing hard. That's exactly backwards. Stepping back locks you on your heels, so you lose your spring and can't move laterally. Gripping tight numbs your hand, so you lose all feel. Swinging hard overruns the ball entirely. The fix isn't bravery — it's technique: soft hand, front foot, short motion. Let's go through each one.

## The Body: Grip, L-Shape, U-Shape, Wrist

The Continental grip is non-negotiable for volleys. Eastern or Semi-Western — your groundstroke grips — will force the face to open up at contact and send the ball long. To find Continental, hold the racket as if it were a hammer, ready to drive a nail. The V between your thumb and index finger should sit on bevel 2, the top-right angled edge of the handle. This grip naturally places the racket face slightly open — perfect for slicing and redirecting — and it's the same grip for both forehand or backhand volleys. No grip change, no wasted time.

The two contact points that matter most are your thumb pad, resting on the back bevel, and your index knuckle, on bevel 2. These two points become your feelers — they read the angle of the face without you having to look at it.

The L-shape is your structural brace. At contact, your wrist and forearm should form a firm L, about 90 to 110 degrees, with the racket head always above your wrist. Thisn't a groundstroke — your wrist isn't loose here. It's frozen: firm but not rigid, like a concrete block rather than a steel bar. The L gives you three things at once: a stable face angle, a braced wrist that absorbs pace instead of flopping, and a lever you can micro-adjust with thumb pressure. The racket head staying above your wrist is the single most important visual cue in the whole shot — if the head drops below the wrist, the face opens and the ball floats long.

Try this mirror test: hold the racket in Continental, bend your wrist back so the racket head is clearly above your hand — that's your L. Now move the racket slightly left and right, as if volleying, and watch the face angle stay almost the same. That's the whole trick. Try it again with the L flattened out and you'll see the face angle swing wildly with every small move. The L is what gives you control.

The U-shape is your ready frame. Between points and during your split-step, your arms form a wide U in front of your chest — your non-dominant hand on the racket throat, or floating opposite the racket, with the racket head at about eye level. This wide U does three things: it keeps the racket in the ready zone so no time gets wasted taking it back, it keeps your shoulders square to the net, and it lets you rotate either shoulder for a forehand or backhand volley without a big move. This the tai chi posture "embrace the moon" or "holding the ball" — a spacious, elastic frame that absorbs force without collapsing.

A common error at 3.5 is letting the racket drop to waist level "to be ready." The problem is that a waist-level racket has to be lifted for every single volley, and the ball's already past you by the time it arrives. Keep the head at eye level — that's the actual ready position.

The wrist works through ulnar deviation, not supination. The wrist action in a volley is ulnar deviation — tilting toward the pinky — plus wrist extension, laying the wrist back to form the L. Supination, rotating the forearm so the palm turns up, is a setup movement that opens the face before the ball arrives, but it shouldn't be active during contact. Active supination at contact turns the shot "wristy" and you lose control. The wrist stays frozen through contact, like a backboard rather than a whip. After contact, it can relax and the hand follows through naturally.

Ulnar deviation is an essential position that keeps the racket head up and stable. Wrist extension is an essential position that maintains the L-shape for firm contact. Supination is setup-only — it opens the face before the ball arrives, but you want to avoid it right at contact.

A locked wrist at 50+ isn't just technique, it's joint protection. Repeated supination under load — the ball pushing the racket back — is the most common cause of tennis elbow on the medial side. A frozen L means the force gets absorbed by the racket and your core, not by your wrist tendons.

## The Feet: Step-In, Hư-Thực, and Body Weight

The split-step is your trigger. The instant your opponent strikes the ball, do a tiny split-step hop — both feet leave the ground a centimeter or two, both knees bend slightly, weight lands on the balls of both feet. This what converts you from standing to ready-to-explode. Without it, you're always one step late and your first move is sluggish. The split-step is tiny — not a jump, not a bounce — it's a loading of the legs, like a compressed spring.

Drill this with a 2-second hold: stand on the service line in ready position, have a partner call "Now!" randomly, do the split-step, and hold the landing for two full seconds. Check that you're on the balls of your feet, your knees are slightly bent, and your racket's at eye level. Do 20 reps.

Hư-Thực — the substantial and insubstantial leg. In tai chi, every step has one substantial leg, full weight, rooted, and one insubstantial leg, light and free to move. You never put full weight on both legs at once — that's "double-weighted" and it leaves you stuck. Applied to volleys: as you step toward the ball, your back leg is the engine that pushes, and your front leg is the bridge that lands and absorbs. About 70% of your weight ends up on the front leg at contact. For a right-hander, the forehand volley has your left foot stepping forward and across toward the ball, becoming the bridge, while your right foot drives. On the backhand volley, your right foot steps across as the bridge while your left foot drives. The bridge leg needs to stay soft — knee bent, glute engaged, ready to absorb the impact of stepping in. A locked bridge leg means a jarring impact straight into your knee and back.

At 50+, step down, not forward, for low balls. When the ball's low, the temptation is to bend at the waist and reach down — that's wrong. Bend at the knees instead, drop the hips, keep the spine neutral, and let the legs do the work. Reaching with a bent back is exactly how a disc gets herniated. Drop with the legs, not the back.

Move forward into the volley. This the most counter-intuitive lesson at 50+. Your brain says "ball coming fast, step back to give myself time." The court says backward is wrong. When you step back, three things die at once: you lose the calf-spring on your heels and can't move laterally, the racket face opens up and the ball floats long, and the contact point moves behind your body, forcing you to reach with the wrist and send the ball into the net. When you step in instead, the calf-spring stays loaded on the balls of your feet for a quick lateral move, your body leans into the ball so the face stays slightly closed for a downward trajectory, and the contact point moves in front of you for a clean strike with real pace.

This your Predator Stance: stand on the balls of your feet, racket at eye level, body leaning slightly forward. You're the hunter, not the target.

Drill this with the "1-2 in": stand two meters behind the service line, have a partner feed a slow ball, split-step on "1," step forward with the appropriate foot on "2," volley, then reset. Repeat 20 times on each side. The goal is training your body that "in" is the default, never "back."

The power step runs 70/30 at contact. At the moment of contact, your weight should distribute roughly 70% on the front leg and 30% on the back leg — This your power step, the actual source of whatever pace your volley carries. You don't need to swing hard, because the body is the engine here, not the arm. When the front leg plants, the back leg pushes, the hip rotates a few degrees, the shoulder fires, and the arm simply delivers that energy to the ball. The arm is a delivery system, not a generator — which is the complete opposite of how most 3.5 players volley. They use the arm as the generator, swinging hard, and treat the body as a passenger. Reverse that.

Across the four phases: at setup, your body has the split-step loaded, both hands sit in the U-shape, and the racket head's at eye level. At the step, your front foot plants at 70%, your hand pushes through with the shoulder, and your L-shape locks in. At contact, your hip rotates about 5 degrees, your wrist stays frozen, and the racket head stays above your wrist. At follow-through, your weight settles on the front foot, you finish short at about six inches, and the racket returns to the ready U.

## The Five Variations

You've got five core volleys. Each uses the same body framework — Continental, L-shape, U-shape, step-in — but differs in how much swing and how much pace it calls for. Master one at a time.

The punch volley is for when the ball comes at medium pace and you want to redirect it firmly. It's a firm, controlled volley with a short punch motion, about 6 to 12 inches of total racket travel. The shoulder fires, the arm extends, the wrist stays locked, and the ball goes back with the pace it arrived with, plus a little extra from your body. The sequence is three steps: split-step, step-in with the opposite foot, punch through to your target. If the ball's at or above net height, hit it firmly cross-court or down the line for a winner.

The block volley is for when the ball comes hard at your body — a passing shot, a return aimed at your torso — and you have no time to do anything except get the racket up. The racket becomes a wall here. Your wrist is locked, your body absorbs the pace, and there's no swing at all — the ball's own energy comes back at your opponent. Position your body behind the ball, turning your shoulder into its path so your chest and stomach aren't exposed. The backhand block is your friend on this one: if the ball's at your body, the backhand side is the safer option, since the back of the racket covers more surface and the elbow can brace out away from the body. Aim cross-court for safety — down the line from a body block is high-risk, since the alley is wide open.

At 50+, This worth a dedicated body-shot drill: have a partner stand at the service line and feed firm balls directly at your chest, and block them back cross-court with a backhand volley, no swing at all. Do 20. The goal is rewiring your brain that "ball at body" isn't a threat, it's just something to block — and it's a confidence drill too, since the more balls you successfully block, the less your body flinches at the next one.

The drop volley is for when your opponent's far behind the baseline and you want to use their own depth against them, forcing them to run forward. This the highest-skill volley in the family — you meet the ball with the face wide open, almost no forward motion, and just let the ball die over the net. Think of the ball as a marble you're placing on a shelf: no force, just placement. A loose grip is essential here — pressure at 2 to 3 out of 10 — so the strings give slightly on contact and kill the pace. The ball should land within about a foot or two of the net on the other side; if it goes higher, you've added too much pace.

The drop volley requires controlling the face angle within two or three degrees while keeping your body weight perfectly still, and most 3.5 players can't reliably do that. The good news is you don't need it to win matches — use it once or twice a match as a surprise weapon. The threat of the drop is often more dangerous than the drop itself.

The lob volley is for when your opponent's at the net and you're also at the net, and you need to defend against being passed by going over their head instead. Open the face, lift the ball high and deep, and let your non-dominant hand reach up to track the ball and balance your shoulders. It works best when you catch the ball at or above shoulder height — if it's lower, switch to a normal lob instead. Aim deep, not just high — a short lob gets smashed, while one landing near the baseline gives you time to recover.

The half-volley is your rescue shot at the net, for when the ball's at your feet and you have no time to let it bounce properly. Bend your knees deeply — drop, don't reach. The racket face stays open, which the Continental grip does naturally, and you make a small forward-and-up scooping motion, hitting the ball the moment it touches the ground. This genuinely hard at 50+ — getting that low with balance and then exploding back up is a real test of your legs. Do ten half-volleys every session and build the leg strength gradually; within three months it starts to feel automatic.

The decision tree at the net, in one pass: if the ball's at or above net height, hit a punch volley, cross-court for safety or down the line for the kill. If it's at your body and fast, block volley, backhand side, cross-court, no swing. If it's below net height but reachable, step in, drop your knees, and slice it deep. If it's at your feet, half-volley, deep knee bend, scooping motion. If it's high with your opponent at net, lob volley, open face, non-dominant hand tracking, aim deep.

## Five Drills for This Week

Drill 1 — Wall Volleys (solo, 5 minutes a day). Stand 2 to 3 meters from a wall and volley continuously, alternating forehand or backhand, with no backswing and no swing at all — just set the face and redirect. Keep the racket head above your wrist, eyes on the ball through contact, soft hands so the strings make a dull thud rather than a sharp clack, and step in a small step for every volley. Goal: 30 consecutive volleys, 15 each side, without losing rhythm.

Drill 2 — Towel Under the Armpit (solo, 5 minutes a day). Tuck a small rolled towel under your hitting-arm armpit and volley normally — the towel must not fall out. If you flick the wrist or swing from the elbow, the towel drops, so this drill forces you to use the shoulder and core to generate the punch motion instead. It's also gentle on the elbow at 50+, since it actively prevents the wrist-flick that causes medial tennis elbow.

Drill 3 — Mirror Check on the L (solo, 2 minutes a day). Stand in front of a mirror, form the L — racket head above wrist, Continental grip — and hold for ten seconds. Check that the V between your thumb and index finger points toward your non-dominant shoulder, that the racket face is slightly open (you should see the strings when looking down at the face), and that you can move the racket six inches left and right without the face angle changing. If the angle changes, your L is collapsing — the grip's too tight or the wrist is bending.

Drill 4 — The V-Step (with a partner, 10 minutes). Stand at the center service line, or at the net if you have access. Your partner feeds balls alternately to your forehand or backhand, 2 to 3 meters in front of you. Run the V-step: split-step, step diagonally forward with the opposite foot, volley, recover to center. Say "one" on the split-step and "two" on the volley — that rhythm makes the footwork automatic. Goal: 20 volleys in 60 seconds, alternating sides, no break in rhythm.

Drill 5 — The Body-Shot Confidence Drill (with a partner, 5 minutes). Your partner stands 4 meters in front of you at the net and throws firm balls directly at your chest, alternating forehand or backhand side. Your job is to block them back cross-court, no swing, just the L-shape absorbing the impact. Start slow with an underhand toss, then progress to firmer throws, then full racket feeds. Goal: 10 successful blocks in a row. This the drill that re-trains your brain that body shots aren't emergencies — the fear response of stepping back, flinching, or panic-swinging is the biggest reason players our age lose points at the net, and repetition is what kills that fear.

## Cues Worth Keeping in Your Head

"L-shape" means the racket head stays above the wrist, wrist locked, both at setup and contact. "Eye-level racket" means the racket head sits at eye level with both hands in the U at your ready position. "Soft hand, bird" is your pressure reminder — 3 to 4 out of 10, like holding a small bird — at setup and contact alike. "In, not back" is your decision-moment rule: step forward into the volley, always. "Knees, not back" is your rule for low balls — bend the knees, not the waist. "70/30" is your weight distribution at contact — 70% on the front leg. "No backswing" applies to every single volley — no wind-up, just set the face. "Six inches" is the maximum forward travel of the racket on a punch volley. "Breathe out" — a small exhale right before contact releases tension. And "hunter, not target" is your mindset on every single ball — lean forward, eyes on the ball, never back off.

## Seven Errors Worth Catching Early

"Slicing down" happens when the racket comes from above and chops into the ball — the fix is keeping the racket head high, with contact happening forward and only a slight downward angle at the very end. "Open face" happens when the wrist flips at contact and the ball floats long — the fix is a frozen L-shape, with supination staying in the setup phase only. "Long backswing" happens when the racket goes back behind the head and you arrive late — the fix is keeping the racket in front, using the "no backswing" cue. "Drop and reach" happens when you bend at the waist for low balls — the fix is dropping the knees and hinging at the hips, not the spine. "Stepping back" happens when your foot moves backward as the ball comes in fast — the fix is the "1-2 in" drill and the Predator Stance. "Flicking the wrist" happens when the wrist snaps at contact chasing extra pace — the fix is the towel-under-armpit drill and a frozen L. And "grip change" happens when you switch grips between forehand or backhand volleys — the fix is remembering Continental covers both, so there's nothing to change.

## Where 50+ Players Actually Get Hurt

Medial tennis elbow usually traces back to wrist-flicking under load — a frozen L absorbs that force in your core instead of your tendons. Knee pain usually comes from deep lunges on hard courts — keep the bridge leg bent, never locked, and favor softer surfaces when you can. Back pain usually comes from reaching forward with a bent spine — use your knees, not your back, hinging at the hip rather than the waist. Shoulder strain usually comes from swinging volleys from the arm — let the shoulder fire the punch while the body actually delivers the power. Slow reactions usually trace back to a split-step that's happening too late, with the body slow to load — a daily two-minute wall drill builds that reflex back up. And fear at the net — the stepping back, the flinch, the panic swing — gets rewired specifically by the body-shot drill, ten reps a session.

If there's one rule I want you to carry out of this whole guide, it's this: you're not trying to win the point from the volley, you're trying to not lose the point from the volley. A blocked ball that stays in play buys you time. A panicked swing into the net just hands your opponent the point. Survival first, then placement, then pace. Always, in that order.

## The Tai Chi Connection

*Phóng Tùng* (放鬆), releasing tension, is your soft hand at 3 to 4 out of 10 pressure, with no tension held in the shoulders. *Thính Kình* (聽勁), listening to force, is feeling the ball's pace and adjusting your face angle accordingly rather than fighting it. *Hư Thực* (虛實), substantial and insubstantial, is your 70/30 weight split between front and back leg at contact. *Lã Kình* (捋勁), rolling back and redirecting, is the entire premise of the volley — redirect the pace, don't generate it. And *Lỗi Công* (掤勁), pushing outward, is your six-inch punch — firm, but never forced.

## Before Every Point — The Mental Checklist

Before the point: racket at eye level in the U-shape, Continental grip with the V pointed toward your non-dominant shoulder, feet shoulder-width apart with weight on the balls of your feet.

When your opponent strikes: split-step — a small hop, knees loaded.

As the ball approaches: step forward with the opposite foot as your bridge leg, set the face early with no backswing, racket head above the wrist in the L-shape.

At contact: wrist frozen, 70% of your weight on the front leg, eyes on the ball, a six-inch punch rather than a swing.

After contact: recover to center, reset the U-shape, and split-step again for the next shot.

The volley isn't the hardest shot in tennis. It's the most different one. It asks you to give up the habit of swinging hard and replace it with setting the face at the right angle — that's a change in mindset, not a change in strength.

Run all five drills twice a week, and within six weeks the volley stops being the thing you dread going to the net for, and starts being the thing you actively want to go to the net for.

Tai chi teaches *Lã Kình* — don't block the force, roll with it. The volley teaches the same lesson: don't hit the ball, redirect it. Here's to more trips to the net, more balls kept in play, and knees that stay healthy along the way.
