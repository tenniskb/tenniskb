# The Backhand: The Mirror, The Whip & The Slice

Watch any good player hit a backhand and you'll notice something: the setup looks just like the forehand. Same unit turn, same chest rotation, same contact out in front. The direction is mirrored — the technique isn't different at all.

That's the mental shift I need you to make, because most recreational players treat the backhand like a completely different stroke. They swing the arm instead of turning the chest. They look at the ball instead of through it. They slow down because the whole thing "feels weird." Here's the truth: the same four-layer system you already know from your forehand — the swing, the setup, the axis, the feel — applies 100% to the backhand. You're just flipping the direction.

Stand in front of a mirror sometime and try it. Hit a forehand. Now mirror it — same turn, same weight shift, same finish, just with the racket on the other side. Your body doesn't actually know "forehand" or "backhand." It only knows "chest turns, arm follows, ball goes." Side is irrelevant.

You've got two places, though, where the mirror image isn't quite exact. First: on a two-handed backhand, your dominant hand is still the engine, but now your non-dominant hand is on the racket too — as a brace, not a driver. The left hand stabilizes, the right hand accelerates. It's the reverse of what feels natural at first. Second: the finish lands over the opposite shoulder. Your forehand finishes over your right shoulder (for a right-hander); your two-handed backhand finishes over the LEFT shoulder. This the single biggest visual tell for whether a backhand is being hit correctly.

Think of forehand or backhand as two pages of the same book. Open to the right and the chapter reads "right shoulder finish." Flip to the left and it reads "left shoulder finish." Same book, mirrored chapter. Most 3.5 players finish their backhand over the same shoulder as their forehand — the right one — and that's exactly wrong. It produces a weak, wristy shot with nothing behind it. The finish is your diagnosis: if your backhand keeps ending over your right shoulder, your chest simply isn't turning enough. Reset the unit turn and start there.

## The Two-Handed Backhand: Your 3.5 Default

The two-handed backhand is the rational choice once you're past 50. It's stable, it's got real power in it, and it's gentle on the shoulder. The kinetic chain runs exactly like your forehand's — swing, setup, axis, feel — just applied to the other side.

Grips. Your dominant hand uses the same grip as your forehand — Semi-Western or Eastern, V on bevel #4 or #3. Your non-dominant hand uses Continental, bevel #2, the same grip you already use for the volley. That left hand is there purely for stability, not to add topspin. Here's why Continental specifically: it puts the racket face in a slightly closed position at contact, which is exactly what a backhand needs. If you used Eastern or Semi-Western on that hand instead, the face would sit too open at contact and the ball would sail long — and this, using the same grip on both hands, is far and away the number one reason recreational backhands float deep and long.

The unit turn. It's the same turn as your forehand, just rotating right instead of left. Chin rests on the right shoulder. Both hands come together to chest height, left hand on the throat of the racket. From that chest-high "diamond," both hands drop together as the shoulders turn, and the racket head stays up and slightly behind — forming a D-shape, or a reverse-C, against your body. Try this check in the mirror: do a forehand unit turn, chin on left shoulder, racket head up. Now do the backhand version, chin on right shoulder, racket head up. They should look identical except for direction. If your backhand turn comes out shallower, slower, or less committed than your forehand turn, that's exactly where your problems start.

The axis. Here's the layer most players skip past, and it matters most. The two-handed backhand is not a two-arm swing — it's a chest rotation with two hands along for the ride. The chest turns, both shoulders move together, and the arms just follow. For a right-hander, your left side becomes the rooted, weighted axis — the "thực" side, if you know the tai chi language — while your right side is free to fly, the "hư" side. It's the exact opposite of your forehand. After contact, both shoulders should square up to the net; if your right shoulder is still pointing off to the side, your chest hasn't finished its job. In tai chi, this rooting-while-rotating idea is called Tọa Kua — sitting into the hip. On the backhand, your left leg roots while your right leg drives. The body rotates around that rooted hip, not around a planted foot.

The swing itself. The L shape lives on the backhand too — the angle between your right forearm and the racket shaft stays bent almost all the way to contact. Straightening it early is the same mistake players make on the forehand, and it's just as costly here. Keep the 70/30 rhythm: 70% of the swing is slow — preparation, the drop, loading the lag — and the final 30% is fast, contact through to finish. The drop mirrors the forehand's too: instead of falling behind your back, the racket drops to your left side, below the wrist, but the principle is identical — the racket head falls on its own edge, you don't pull it down with your arm. Think of it as a fisherman's rod-and-flick, mirrored: on the forehand the rod is your right arm and the flick is your right shoulder; on the backhand the rod is your left arm, because both hands are on the racket, and the flick is your chest and both shoulders turning together. The fisherman is your torso, not your arm.

Contact happens at full extension, in front of your lead foot, roughly waist-to-chest height. The L releases as your right forearm pronates slightly to add topspin, and the racket finishes high over your LEFT shoulder — the single most important visual check for a correct two-handed backhand. Total follow-through travel is about 1.5 meters, shorter than the forehand's, with both hands staying together the whole way. One trap to watch for: players try to snap the wrist on the backhand to add pace. Don't. The two-handed backhand runs on almost no wrist action at all — the power is chest rotation, full stop. A wristy backhand is also a fast road to tennis elbow in that non-dominant brace hand.

Two drills worth building this week. First, the D-Loop Hold: stand in front of a mirror, do a backhand unit turn, both hands together at chest height, both shoulders turned right, racket head up — and hold that D-shape for two full seconds. Check yourself: chin on right shoulder? Racket head up? Both hands together? Elbows down? Ten reps a day teaches your body that the backhand setup deserves the same commitment as the forehand. Second, the Left-Shoulder Finish Check: have a friend watch ten of your backhands with one job only — tell you which shoulder the racket finishes over. If eight out of ten land over your left shoulder, your chest is turning correctly. If most land over the right, you're arm-swinging, not chest-turning. This genuinely the single best diagnostic for the two-handed backhand — use it often.

## The One-Handed Backhand: A Quick Word

The one-handed backhand might be the most beautiful stroke in tennis — Wawrinka, Federer, Dimitrov, Gasquet, Thiem all hit it differently, and all hit it beautifully. It's also the most shoulder-stressful stroke demanding precise timing, real footwork, and a shoulder that can absorb repeated one-sided load.

Past 50, the two-handed backhand is the rational default. This section is really for two groups: players who already have a one-hander and want to refine it, and players who want to add the SLICE one-hander specifically — which is far friendlier on the shoulder than the topspin version.

Three pros, three models of the same stroke. Dimitrov's fluid C-loop drops the racket head below the ball in a large "C," with power coming from hip rotation and the elastic recoil of that shape — it looks like silk. Wawrinka's explosive linear model pulls the left elbow deep behind the back with an aggressive early prep and a 45°-angled front foot; power comes from the torso unwinding, not the arm — it sounds like a cannon. Federer's efficient compact model uses a small "slot" takeback with the racket head below the ball, a nearly straight arm at contact, heel-to-toe footwork, and remarkable head stability — a "quiet eye" that holds on the ball for about 0.2 seconds after contact. His power comes from precision, not size.

Honestly, none of these are exactly right for you — each pro has 15+ years of specialized training behind their version. What's worth borrowing: Dimitrov's feeling of letting the racket head fall, which is an easy cue to copy; Federer's quiet eye and heel-to-toe footwork, which are pure efficiency and protect both your shoulder and your legs. Wawrinka's deep left-elbow pull is the rotational engine behind his shot, but it's genuinely risky on a 50+ shoulder — best left admired on television rather than copied on court.

Your grip here is Continental, no exceptions — Eastern or Semi-Western will force the face open at contact and send the ball long. Footwork should follow Federer's heel-to-toe pattern: the right foot strikes heel-first, then rolls onto the toe as you load, giving you a stable, gradual, controlled step rather than a lunge. And build the quiet eye habit everywhere: after contact, keep your eyes on the contact point for about 0.2 seconds, head barely moving. It costs nothing and it's arguably the cheapest performance upgrade available in tennis.

Ask your shoulder before you ask your ego. If your hitting shoulder carries any history of impingement, rotator cuff strain, or chronic tightness, stay with the two-handed backhand for anything that needs power, and save the one-hander for slice only. In tai chi terms, the one-handed backhand asks the Dantian — your body's movement hub in the lower abdomen — to initiate the rotation, and the Mingmen, the "back power gate" around your lower spine, to load the spring and release it through the hips. More than any other stroke, the one-hander needs both working together.

## The Slice Backhand: Your Workhorse After 50

If there's one shot in this whole guide I'd bet on being under-used in your game, it's the slice. It stays low, buys you time, disrupts your opponent's rhythm, is gentle on your shoulder, and works everywhere — defense, low balls, approach shots, returning big serves. Even if your two-handed backhand is rock solid, there will be days your shoulder, elbow, or back just says "not today." On those days, the slice is the shot that keeps you on the court.

Grip is Continental on both hands for the two-handed slice, or Continental on the single hitting hand for the elegant one-handed version, Federer-style. Either way, the face sits open "like a hood" — tilted up roughly 30-45° from vertical, as if it were the hood of a car angled toward the sky.

The takeback follows a "high takeback, low finish" pattern I like to call "open the door, walk through." The racket goes up high, above the shoulder, like opening a door — and finishes low, across the body, near the opposite hip, like the door swinging shut. Contact happens on the way down. Here's the single most important cue in this whole section: slice means getting UNDER the ball, not over it. The racket path runs high to low, meeting the ball on its underside, giving it backspin and sidespin that keeps it low after the bounce. Picture a windshield wiper — high point at one end of the arc, low finish at the other, contact somewhere in the middle on the way down, face angled the entire time.

Contact lands around waist height, slightly in front of your lead foot, on the way down — you're cutting the ball from underneath. The finish is low, across your body, racket head ending up near the opposite hip, with your weight already transferred forward. And here's a genuine paradox worth internalizing: soft hands, firm contact. Your grip pressure stays loose, maybe a 3 or 4 out of 10, but the contact itself is firm — the strings don't give much on impact. Relaxed hand, solid impact, both at once.

Three mistakes show up constantly at this level. Swinging up at the ball produces a floater — the slice path is down, never up. Closing the face at contact sends the ball diving into the net — the face needs to stay open all the way through. And swinging too hard misses the point entirely; slice is a placement shot, not a power shot, so let the underspin do the work instead of your arm.

Four situations, four cues worth memorizing. When you're under pressure, defending: just get it back deep, buying time to recover — no pace needed. On an approach shot: slice low and move forward, since a low ball forces your opponent to slice or lob back, opening up the net for your volley. Returning a big serve: slice is your friend, because the underspin absorbs their pace and lands short, taking time away from the server. And after a topspin rally, throwing in a slice disrupts the rhythm entirely — different spin, different timing, and your opponent's clock gets thrown off.

Two drills for building the slice. The 20-Slice Wall Rally: stand 4 meters from a wall and rally backhand slice only, slow pace, aiming for 20 consecutive balls that bounce below waist height on your side. Listen for the sound — a clean slice sounds like a soft "fwoosh," a flat hit sounds like a hard "thwack." If 20 is too easy, back up to 5 meters; if even 10 feels hard, move in to 3. The Slice-Then-Forward Approach: rally topspin-to-topspin with a partner, and after four balls, hit a slice instead — the instant your racket touches the ball, split-step and move forward toward the net. It works because the low slice forces your opponent into a defensive slice or lob of their own, buying you the time to close in. Aim for 5 out of 10 successful approaches where you actually reach the net and volley the next ball.

## Cues, Errors, and the Reality Check for Players Past 50

A short list of cues worth carrying onto the court: mirror the forehand — same turn, same drop, same finish, just reversed. Dominant hand drives, non-dominant hand braces. Non-dominant grip is Continental, always. Chest turns and both shoulders move together. Elbow leads, wrist stays locked — no snap. Left-shoulder finish is your target. Keep the 70-slow, 30-fast rhythm. Remember that slice means high to low with an open face. Soft hands, firm contact. And build the quiet eye into every single shot, not just the backhand.

Seven mistakes turn up again and again at 3.5. Using the same grip on both hands leaves the face too open — fix it with Continental specifically on the non-dominant hand. Finishing over the right shoulder means you're arm-swinging instead of chest-turning — the chest-turn drill and the left-shoulder finish check both address this directly. A wrist snap at contact is both weak and risky for the elbow — cue "elbow leads" and try holding a towel under your armpit during practice swings to keep the wrist quiet. Swinging up on a slice produces a floater — remember "slice means under the ball," and check yourself against a wall. A closed face on a slice sends it into the net — cue "open like a hood" and check your Continental grip. Arm-swinging in general — both arms pushing while the chest stays still — gets fixed with "chest turns, arms follow" and some shadow-swing practice. And a shallow unit turn, smaller or later than your forehand's, responds well to the D-Loop Hold drill and regular mirror checks.

Now, the honest conversation about your body past 50. A one-handed topspin backhand loads the shoulder repeatedly on one side — use the two-hander for power shots and save the one-hander for slice only. Your non-dominant brace hand can develop tennis elbow if the grip is too tight under load — loosen it to that 3-4 out of 10 and stop squeezing. Radial deviation — the wrist bending toward the thumb on a two-hander — gets fixed with "elbow leads" and cutting out the wrist flick entirely. Excessive shoulder turn on a one-hander can twist the lower back — lean on the two-hander for wide balls and save the one-hander for neutral ones. Deep knee bends on low slices can aggravate the knees — keep a bridge leg bent rather than dropping hard, and favor softer surfaces when you have the choice. And the biggest one: don't let fear of your backhand side create hesitation and late contact. Three minutes a day of mirror work, and real trust in the same L1-L4 system you already know, fixes that over time.

If there's a single rule to take from all of this, it's this: the backhand is not your weak side. It's your mirror. Treat it with exactly the same respect as your forehand — same commitment to the unit turn, same four-layer system, same finish line. The moment you start treating the backhand as "the shot I don't like," you've handed away 30% of the court.
