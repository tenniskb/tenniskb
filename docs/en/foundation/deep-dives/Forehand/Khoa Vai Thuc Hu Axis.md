# The Locked-Shoulder Axis: Strong Side, Free Side

*The rotational core that sits between your setup and your swing*

Tai chi has a principle that took about two hundred years to work out fully: in every movement, one side of the body is "solid" — the anchor — and the other is "empty" — the free one. They switch back and forth, but they never both work at the same time.

Tennis works exactly the same way. On a forehand, your left shoulder is the solid anchor holding the rotation together, and your right shoulder is the free one doing the swinging. On a backhand, they switch roles entirely.

Recreational players do the opposite — they try to use both shoulders to swing at once. That's the number one reason behind "I swung hard but the ball went nowhere." **The strong side makes the axis. The free side makes the whip.**

Think of a door. The hinge is the solid side — it doesn't move, but it holds everything together. The door itself is the free side — it swings freely precisely because the hinge is locked. Your spine is the hinge. Your racket arm is the door. A wobbly hinge means a wobbly door. A solid hinge means the door flies.

## Frozen Shoulder, Flying Shoulder

On a forehand, once your unit turn is complete, your left shoulder freezes. It points toward the side fence and doesn't come around. Your right shoulder is the one that flies — but it only flies because the left one is locked in place. Try to swing both shoulders forward together, and you lose your axis entirely — you end up muscling the ball instead of whipping it.

Picture a clock face on your chest. Twelve is straight up, three is to the right, six is down, nine is to the left. After your unit turn on a forehand, the twelve on your chest points toward the right side fence. As you swing, that twelve rotates to face the ball. Your spine is the pin at the center of the clock — it doesn't move. Your chest is the clock face — it rotates around that pin.

### The 70/30 Weight Split

On a forehand, about 70% of your weight sits on your back leg — your right leg, if you're right-handed. That back leg is the solid, planted, coiled spring. Your front leg is the bridge — slightly bent, receiving the rotation, but not pushing.

The transfer of weight from back to front happens after contact, not before.

The mistake I see constantly is transferring weight too early. Step forward before contact, and you lose your back leg's stored power — the leg straightens, the spring releases too soon, and you end up pushing the ball with your arm instead. Let the back leg stay bent through contact. Transfer after, not before.

## The Axis: Your Spine Is a Door Hinge, Not a Barrel

The axis is the line everything rotates around. Most players think that axis is the lower back. It isn't. **The axis is the spine, and your lower back should stay relatively still.**

Think of your spine as a flagpole. Your chest is a banner. Your arms are ribbons. The flagpole doesn't bend. The banner catches the wind. The ribbons fly. That's the whole locked-shoulder principle: the left shoulder stays anchored to the spine while the right shoulder flies freely around it.

**Try this — The Towel Test.** Tuck a small towel under your left armpit (for a right-hander hitting forehand). Do a slow forehand. The towel shouldn't fall. If it falls, your left shoulder came forward — the axis broke, and you swung with your arm instead of your body. If it stays put, your left shoulder held its position — the axis held, and your chest rotated properly around your spine.

Why is the left side the anchor for a right-handed forehand? Your left leg sits closer to the net. Your left arm is your non-dominant arm. Both are naturally better at holding than swinging. Use them as anchors, not active swingers. The dominant side is the whip. The non-dominant side is the post.

**The diamond frame.** This is the shape formed by your chest, both shoulders, and both hands when you hold the racket in front of you. It should stay intact through the unit turn — the diamond is the load-bearing structure of your whole swing. It breaks the moment your left hand drops or your left shoulder rotates forward early. Break the diamond, and your swing becomes an arm swing. Hold it, and your swing becomes a body swing.

Imagine a plate of glass glued to your chest. Your left hand and the racket throat hold that plate in place. Your right hand holds the grip. As long as the plate doesn't fall, your chest is solid. The moment it falls, the diamond's broken.

**The side-tilt.** Most players think rotation is just "turning the shoulders." It's not — real rotation includes a slight side-tilt, your upper body leaning a touch toward the ball as your hips rotate. That's the coiled-spring effect: the tilt pre-loads one side of your body like a spring being compressed, and releasing it fires the spring. The cue: "pour water out of your right hip," like you're tipping a glass sideways.

Watch a baseball pitcher — their upper body tilts dramatically toward the plate before release. That's the same side-tilt, loading a spring. You're doing the same thing on a forehand, just smaller.

## The Three Tiers: Top Stable, Middle Soft, Bottom Springy

Your body works in three tiers, and each has a different job. Top tier — chest and shoulders — stays stable. Middle tier — waist and hips — stays soft. Bottom tier — your legs — stays springy.

Most recreational players get this backward: a soft, wobbly chest, a locked-tight waist, and stiff, straight legs. The result is no power and a lot of arm effort. The fix is simple, if a little counterintuitive: tighten the top, loosen the middle, bend the bottom.

**Top — stable.** Your chest is the solid side of your upper body. After the unit turn, it should be open sideways, facing the side fence. Your left shoulder (on a forehand) is the anchor — it points to the side fence and doesn't come around until after contact. Your right shoulder flies, but only around that locked left side.

Imagine a button on your chest. After the unit turn, that button faces the side fence. As you swing, it rotates to face the ball — but your spine stays vertical the entire time. Spine: vertical. Chest: rotating. Two different jobs.

**Middle — soft.** Your waist and hips act as the body's shock absorber. A tight waist stops the rotation dead at the hips, forcing your upper body to swing on its own. The cue: soft belly, loose hips. A slightly soft midsection lets the rotation flow smoothly from the bottom tier up into the top tier. This is also where the free side of the rotation lives — your right hip (on a forehand) rotates forward freely, while the left hip stays the anchor.

Imagine your midsection as a glass of water. A rigid glass makes the water slosh violently. A slightly flexible one lets it move smoothly. A slightly soft waist means smooth rotation. A tight waist means a violent jerk.

**Bottom — springy.** Your legs are the engine. The back leg is solid — a deeply bent, coiled spring. The front leg is the bridge — slightly bent. The back leg pushes into the ground, the ground pushes back, and that force travels up through the hips, through the soft middle, into the stable top. That's the kinetic chain — the famous "power comes from the ground" idea, in practice.

Your back leg is a coiled spring — the deeper you bend it, the more energy you store; the straighter you keep it, the less you have available. But never lock the knee. Coiled doesn't mean locked.

## The Switch: Forehand Locks Left, Backhand Locks Right

This is where most players at your level get confused. The anchor side switches depending on which wing you're hitting.

**Forehand (right-hander):** left shoulder locked, right shoulder free. Back leg is the right leg — deeply bent, coiled.

**One-handed backhand (right-hander):** right shoulder locked, left shoulder free. Back leg is the left leg — deeply bent, coiled.

The pattern: the shoulder away from the swing is the locked one. The shoulder on the swing side is the free one.

For the two-handed backhand, your dominant hand still drives the swing, but your non-dominant hand becomes the solid support — both hands lock together as one unit, with the right shoulder acting as the anchor.

The simple rule to remember: **solid is away from the racket. Free is on the racket side.**

**Drill — The Switch Test.** Stand in ready position. Do a forehand unit turn — notice your left shoulder locking. Reset. Do a backhand unit turn — notice your right shoulder locking. Do five forehands, five backhands, alternating. The locked shoulder should switch cleanly every single time. Feel that switch happening, and you understand the axis. If both shoulders keep trying to swing, you don't yet.

## The 50+ Adjustments: Save the Lower Back, Save the Shoulders

This locked-shoulder axis is especially good for players past 50, because it routes force through the hips — a large joint built for rotation — instead of the lower back, a stack of discs that was never designed to twist. But it has to be done correctly. Done wrong, it loads the rotator cuff. Done right, it protects both your back and your shoulders.

**Rule one — the "locked" shoulder is soft, not stiff.** Locking the shoulder doesn't mean tensing it. It means the shoulder stays in place while the chest rotates around it. Your deltoid and rotator cuff muscles should stay relaxed — they're holding your arm in position, not pushing anything. Feel your shoulder muscles working hard? The rotation is going through them instead of your chest. Stop, reset, try again.

Try this: after the unit turn, have a friend lift your right arm — the free side. It should feel light, almost like a dead fish. If it feels heavy and tense instead, your arm is active when it should be completely passive.

**Rule two — the lower back stays vertical.** During rotation, your lower back should stay vertical, or very slightly tilted — never curved sideways. The rotation happens above the lower back, in the chest, and below it, in the hips. Your lower back is just the stable column connecting the two. Feel it curving or twisting? The rotation is going through the wrong place.

Try this: stand with your back against a wall and do a slow forehand. Your lower back shouldn't lose contact with the wall. If it does, the rotation is running through your back instead of your hips — and that's exactly where tennis back injuries come from.

**Rule three — the hips lead, never follow.** The hips start the rotation. The chest follows. The arms follow the chest. The racket follows the arms. It's a chain, in that exact order. If the arms swing first, the chain breaks — the hips are the engine here, not the brakes. Past 50, this matters even more, because your lower back will quietly compensate if the hips don't lead.

Imagine your hips as a tow truck, your chest as the car being towed, and your arms as the rope connecting them. The tow truck moves first, the car follows, and the rope just goes along for the ride. Reverse that order, and the rope snaps.

## Four Drills for This Week

**1. The Towel Under the Armpit** — daily, 3 minutes. Tuck a small towel under your left armpit. Twenty slow forehands. The towel shouldn't fall. This trains the locked-shoulder feeling directly. Goal: 20 clean reps, no falling towel.

**2. The Diamond Hold** — daily, 2 minutes. Ready position, both hands together in front of your chest, racket in the middle. Hold for two seconds. This trains the diamond frame — the load-bearing structure that holds through rotation. Goal: 10 clean reps, eyes closed, two seconds each.

**3. The Hips-First Rotation** — 3 times a week, 5 minutes. Feet shoulder-width apart, holding a light object (racket or broom) in both hands at chest height. Rotate by moving the hips first — the chest follows, the arms follow the chest. It should feel like a chain reaction. Goal: 20 reps, focused on the order, not the speed.

**4. The Wall Check** — as a warmup, 2 minutes. Back against a wall, slow forehand. Your lower back shouldn't lose contact with the wall. Losing contact means the rotation is going through your back instead of your hips. This is your 50+ safety check — if you can't pass it, you're not ready to hit yet. Goal: 10 clean reps a day as warmup.

## The Six Cues to Remember

1. **Solid is away from the racket. Free is on the racket side.**
2. **Frozen shoulder, flying shoulder.**
3. **Spine stays vertical. Chest rotates. Two different jobs.**
4. **Tighten the top, loosen the middle, bend the bottom.**
5. **Hips first, chest second, arms third, racket last.**
6. **Locked means soft, not stiff.**

## Five Warnings Worth Repeating

1. Don't tense the "locked" shoulder — it stays back by relaxing, not by tightening.
2. Don't rotate through the lower back — keep it vertical. Rotation runs through the hips.
3. Don't transfer weight before contact — let the back leg stay bent through the swing.
4. Don't swing both shoulders at once — one locked, one free, always.
5. Don't lock the front knee — it's a bridge, slightly bent, not a pillar.

## How This Connects to the Rest

The unit turn is your loading position — this piece explains how that loading actually works. The L-Angle swing is what happens once the axis is doing its job — the whip flies because the axis underneath it is solid. And the feel of this axis becomes automatic with enough practice — eventually you stop thinking about it and your body just does it.

## The 50+ Reality Check

If you only take one technique from this whole series, take this one. It uses your hip joint — large, durable, built for rotation. It protects your lower back, which was never built for rotation. And it protects your shoulder, because the shoulder stays soft instead of tight throughout. Of everything in this series, this is the most 50-plus-friendly piece by a wide margin.
