# Foundations & Grip: Your Interface With Energy

This one's about the most basic thing and the thing most players never actually think about: how your hand meets the handle. At your stage, the priority isn't just power — it's structural integrity. Your grip isn't just how you hold the racket. It's how you connect your entire kinetic chain to the ball.

## The Grip Philosophy

Here's something most players never realize: your grip is a signal to your brain, not just a hand position. Grip too rigidly, and your brain reads that tightness as danger and restricts your movement to protect the joint. Keep the grip adaptive instead, and your nervous system stays fluid — which is exactly what lets the whip effect happen in the first place.

Think of your grip as an interface, not a handle. Pressure should sit around 3 out of 10 during the swing itself, then rise to about 9 out of 10 right at impact. And the bevels of the handle are what actually define which shot you're set up to hit.

### The Three Master Grips

- Continental (bevel 2) — the universal grip. Covers your volley, slice, and serve. It's the most neutral interface you have.
- Eastern/Semi-Western (bevel 3-4) — the standard forehand grip at your level. Gives you a stable face at contact.
- Western (bevel 5+) — extreme spin, but it demands a lot physically, and it's often risky on the wrists past 50.

## Structural Integrity

The stability of your forearm through a swing depends entirely on how your knuckles and heel pad line up with the racket's bevels. Get that alignment wrong, and your small wrist muscles end up compensating for it — which is exactly the kind of repeated strain that turns chronic over time.

Place your heel pad — the base of your thumb — right on the bevel. Feel the weight settle into your base knuckles. And avoid the "death grip" entirely — that white-knuckle squeeze is one of the fastest ways to aggravate your elbow.

## The Contact Point Architecture

Your grip dictates where your ideal contact point actually is. Mix grips and swing paths — say, a Western grip but an Eastern-style contact point out in front — and you'll shank the ball more often than not, because the two are built for different points in space.

With Eastern or Semi-Western, contact happens in front of you and slightly to the side. With Continental, contact stays out in front too, but it's what allows the open-face carving motion behind a good slice or volley.

## Drills and Mental Cues

The Check-Bevel Drill. Before every point, glance down at your grip. Make sure your knuckle is actually sitting on the bevel it should be. It takes two seconds and it catches drift before it costs you a point.

The Soft-Hand Drill. Hold the racket with just your thumb and index finger and swing gently. This forces you to feel the racket's weight honestly, without any squeezing to hide behind.

### Quick Reference

| Situation | Grip | Contact point | Mental cue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forehand | Semi-Western | In front | "Feel the face" |
| Volley | Continental | In front / to the side | "Block, don't swing" |
| Serve | Continental | Overhead | "Snap, don't push" |

One more diagnostic worth knowing: listen to the contact sound. A solid "thwack" means clean contact. A soft, dull "thud" means a lazy block. Use that sound to calibrate how much tension you're actually carrying in your hand.
