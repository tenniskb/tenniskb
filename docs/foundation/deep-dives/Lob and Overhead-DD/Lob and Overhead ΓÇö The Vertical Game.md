# Lob and Overhead — The Vertical Game

Watch any point at your club that ends in four shots or fewer. Watch the geometry of it. The shot that actually ends the point is almost never a flat cross-court groundstroke. It's almost always a shot that went up or down — a high lob the opponent simply can't reach, or a smash they can only block weakly. Recreational tennis gets dominated by the horizontal game — cross-court, down-the-line, angled. But the vertical game — high balls, lobs, smashes — is what actually decides points, especially once your opponents are skilled enough to retrieve everything you send side to side.

This is about that vertical game. Two shots, really one idea: the lob, where you hit the ball up, and the overhead, where they've hit it up and you put it back down.

Think of it like a chess board. Horizontal moves — left, right — gain you space. Vertical moves change the rules of the game entirely. A lob doesn't just put the ball in the corner — it tells your opponent: you must hit the next shot from below your shoelaces, head craned up, standing still or moving backward. That's a fundamentally different problem than returning a flat ball while balanced at net. That's exactly why lobs and overheads decide points the way flat rallies rarely do.

## The Four Situations and When Each One Applies

Most 3.5 players treat the lob and the overhead as unrelated strokes. They're not — they're paired answers to the same underlying problem: the ball is high, or your opponent is at net. Here's the full decision tree.

Situation one: you're at the baseline, your opponent's at net, and you're under pressure. The answer is the defensive slice lob — it clears their head, lands deep in the corner, and buys you time to recover. Situation two: you're at the baseline, your opponent's at the baseline too, and you want to attack. The answer is the offensive topspin lob — it dips back down inside the court, forcing your opponent into a high ball on the run. Situation three: your opponent hits you a high ball — a lob, a moonball, a simple floater. The answer is the overhead. The ball's already in the air; this is where you finish the point. Situation four: you're at net and your opponent lobs over you. The answer is either an overhead from the net, if you have time to get back and set up, or a lob volley if you genuinely can't get back — your emergency parachute.

Here's the trap most 3.5 players fall into: they only ever practice situation four's lob volley, and never really work on situation one's defensive lob or situation three's overhead. They panic on every high ball. They hit a weak floating "moon" lob when a deep slice lob was called for. They flail wildly at overheads instead of setting up. The whole point of this guide is practicing all four situations — starting with the defensive lob and the controlled overhead, since those are the two you'll use most.

There's a tai chi lens worth knowing here too. *Hư Thực* — emptiness and substance — describes the pair perfectly: a lob creates emptiness behind your opponent, and an overhead exploits a real, substantial high ball. Together they form a complete cycle — you create the space, then you fill it. *Cung Bộ*, the bow stance, is literally the overhead stance — front foot forward, back foot anchored, weight loaded, exactly like the tai chi position of the same name. *Tỉnh Kình*, quiet power, describes a good lob perfectly: minimum racket speed, maximum arc — and a good overhead is the same, minimum effort, maximum placement. Neither shot is a "swing hard" shot. And *Lã Kình*, yielding force, is the defensive lob exactly: you yield to your opponent's pressure and redirect it upward, over their head, instead of meeting it head-on.

Here's the honest framing for 50+ players: the lob and the overhead are genuinely the two most longevity-friendly shots in tennis. The lob is a slow, low-impact motion — no shoulder load, no violent rotation. The overhead uses gravity as its engine — you don't need to swing hard, just point the racket at the ball and let it fall. Both shots protect your joints compared to a grinding baseline rally, which is exactly why they're worth leading with.

## The Lob — Your Insurance Policy

The lob is the most under-appreciated shot in recreational tennis. Not because it's flashy — it isn't — but because it genuinely works. A 50+ player who can hit a deep, arcing defensive lob into the opponent's weaker corner will save more points across a season than a player with a booming 70 mph forehand and no lob at all. The lob is slow on your body, devastating on theirs, and it asks essentially nothing of your shoulder. In tai chi terms, it's *Lã Kình* — rollback energy: your opponent pushes you deep, and you simply redirect that energy up and over their head instead of fighting it.

There are two lobs worth knowing, and I'd strongly recommend leading with the first.

**The defensive slice lob is your default.** Use it when you're under pressure at the baseline and your opponent's at net. Grip: Continental — the same grip you already use for the volley, and genuinely the single most important decision in this whole shot. Switch to Eastern or Semi-Western and the face opens too much at contact; the lob sails long. Stance: open or semi-open, front foot already angled slightly toward the sideline rather than pointed straight at the net — this lets your chest rotate upward into the shot instead of into the ball the way a groundstroke would. Backswing: short and genuinely high. Take the racket back the way you would on a serve — head up, elbow bent, as though you're about to scratch the middle of your own back with the racket. That high backswing is what actually gives the lob its arc; a low one just produces a flat shot that turns into an easy overhead for your opponent.

The "scratch your back" cue is worth internalizing fully: stand ready, lift the racket as if to scratch the middle of your back, and that's your lob backswing. The higher it goes, the higher your arc — and that's a genuinely good thing. Most 3.5 players end up hitting a weak "moon" lob precisely because they keep the backswing low and then try to lift the ball with the wrist instead. Don't lift with the wrist. Lift with the backswing itself.

At contact, catch the ball in front of your body, slightly higher than you would on a normal groundstroke — chest-to-shoulder height on the forehand side, a touch lower, waist-to-chest, on the backhand. The face should sit slightly open — Continental does this naturally — as though it's gently cupping water. A flat face produces a flat shot, which is really just an invitation for your opponent to smash it. The swing path runs low to high, brushing up the back of the ball, more vertical than a normal groundstroke — picture drawing a straight vertical line from your knee to your head, and that's roughly your path. One more piece worth adding: shrug the hitting shoulder up toward your ear right at contact. It adds six to twelve inches of extra height to the arc, and because the motion is gentle rather than violent, it doesn't strain the shoulder at all.

A quick mirror check to lock this in: stand in your open stance, do the "scratch your back" backswing, imagine the ball at chest height in front of you, sweep the racket from knee to head with the face slightly open and a shoulder shrug at the top. Your racket should finish above your head, almost like a mini-serve. Practice the air swing ten times before you ever add a ball.

Where you aim matters as much as the technique. Land the lob within two to three feet of the baseline, near either sideline. A lob landing in the middle of the service box just hands your opponent an easy overhead; one landing in the corner forces them to either sprint twenty-plus feet to retrieve it or hit a desperate, off-balance overhead instead. There's a useful ceiling here too: the minimum arc clears your opponent's reach by about two feet; the maximum useful arc just clears the net by three to four feet on its way up. Anything higher than that just wastes energy and gives your opponent time to reset underneath it. And if you know their backhand overhead is the shakier side, aim there specifically — the lob's job isn't just clearing them, it's making the overhead itself genuinely difficult.

Before you commit to the shot, run a quick test: will this ball clear the highest point of their reach, and will it land within about three feet of the baseline? If both answers are yes, hit it. If either is no, add more arc with a bigger shoulder shrug, or aim deeper into the court. A lob that fails that test is really just a free point handed to your opponent.

**The offensive topspin lob is the braver, riskier cousin — treat it as a reference shot, not your default.** It's the inverse of the defensive lob in almost every way, though the underlying idea — clearing your opponent's head — stays the same. Grip: your regular groundstroke grip, Semi-Western or Eastern, since the face needs to close over the top of the ball to generate real topspin. Contact is lower and more aggressive, around waist height, brushing up the back of the ball from roughly 7 o'clock to 1 o'clock on the forehand (5 to 11 on the backhand). The trajectory is lower and lands inside the court rather than deep — clearing the net by just one to two feet, with the topspin making the ball kick up hard after the bounce, which is exactly what makes it so uncomfortable for your opponent to handle. Use it specifically against a baseline opponent hitting weak, loopy balls — it's a genuine counter-attack, not a defensive shot.

The trap here is a common one: trying to hit the offensive lob with a Continental grip. That just produces a slice lob, not a topspin one — Continental can't generate the brushing motion topspin needs. If you're going to attempt this shot at all, you need your actual groundstroke grip, and you need to swing meaningfully faster than you would on the defensive lob. This is a real groundstroke that happens to go up, not a soft touch shot.

At 50+, here's the honest advice: the lob is your best friend precisely because it's the lowest-stress, highest-percentage shot available to you in tennis — no shoulder load, no violent rotation, no sprint, no sudden stop. Just grip, backswing, lift, deep aim. Spend two full months on the defensive slice lob before you even try the offensive version. Your shoulder, and your match record both, will thank you for the patience.

## The Overhead — The Sane Smash

The overhead smash is the most under-hit shot in recreational tennis. A high ball comes in, the player panics, scrambles in a small circle, and either shanks it into the fence or floats a weak moonball back. Here's the truth that changes everything once it clicks: the overhead is a serve that comes to you. Same grip — Continental. Same contact point — racket head above the ball, fully extended. Same finish — over the non-dominant shoulder. The only real difference is the ball's already in the air instead of being tossed by your own hand. Once you see the overhead as "a serve that already happened," the panic tends to just evaporate. You genuinely have all the time in the world. You don't need to swing hard. You just need to point the racket at the ball and let gravity do the rest.

There are four reasons recreational players consistently miss this shot, worth naming directly. First, they don't get into position early enough — the ball's high, they're still at the baseline, the lob keeps rising, and they arrive at the last second just as it peaks. You need to be in position *before* the ball reaches its peak, which means tracking it from the moment it leaves your opponent's racket, not after. Second, they look straight up at the sky — the instant your head tilts back, your body starts leaning back with it, and that lean takes your legs out of the shot entirely, even though the legs are really the engine of the whole overhead. Keep your head still, eyes on the ball, chin level throughout. Third, they try to "swing" the overhead the way they would a groundstroke — but a groundstroke generates pace through swinging, while an overhead generates pace by falling; the racket drops from a high position and gravity does the actual work. No real swing is needed, just point and release. Fourth, they use the wrong grip — Eastern or Semi-Western leaves the face too open at contact and the ball sails long. Continental is mandatory here, exactly as it is on the serve.

Here's the mental model worth keeping: every overhead is a serve your opponent just tossed for you. You have all the time in the world to set up. The ball's in the air, descending at a comfortable pace. The net's the same height, the court the same shape. The only real difference from your own serve is that you're intercepting instead of tossing. Treat it that way, and your success rate genuinely tends to jump from something like 30% to 80%.

The full sequence, called the Sane Smash, breaks into clear steps. Tracking and stance first: the instant your opponent makes contact with the lob, side-shuffle so the ball will land one to two feet in front of your lead foot — that's the sweet spot. Turn sideways to the ball once you're in position, non-dominant shoulder pointing toward it, which puts you in a genuine serve-ready stance. Plant both feet, knees slightly bent — this is *Cung Bộ*, the bow stance: front foot at 45° to the net, back foot roughly parallel, weight sitting 60/40 back-to-front. And lift both arms into the trophy position — non-dominant hand pointing up at the ball as though tracking a toss, racket arm lifting with the head above and the elbow bent. If you've ever hit a serve, your arms already know this position; the overhead is just the next rep of something you've already trained.

At contact, reach to full extension with the racket head above the ball — the highest comfortable point of your reach, the ball caught between the strings and gravity. The face sits slightly closed, going down and through the ball, exactly like a serve; Continental puts it in the right position naturally. The ball travels down because of two things working together: the downward racket path and gravity itself. The real cue here is worth repeating: instead of "smash it," think "put it down." You're not generating the pace — gravity's already doing that for you. You're simply placing the ball into the court. For aim, cross-court is your safer, more forgiving choice, with more net clearance and margin; down-the-line is the more aggressive "winner" option, since it's a shorter path to the sideline. At 3.5, lead with cross-court. Finish over the non-dominant shoulder, racket head pointing down and back, same as a serve — if your finish ends up over the dominant shoulder or out in front of your body, that's a sign your chest simply didn't turn enough.

Watch a pro hit this shot and you'll notice the racket head starts high, the ball's below it, and the racket essentially falls through the ball. The whole thing happens in well under half a second — there's genuinely no time to swing, only time to point and let go. What looks like a "swing" is really just the racket continuing its natural downward path after contact's already happened. Less is more here, by a wide margin.

The trap to watch for: recreational players often try to muscle this shot, gripping tight and swinging hard, and the ball ends up flying long or into the net. The Sane Smash is the opposite of that instinct entirely — grip pressure sitting around 3-4 out of 10, swing speed at maybe 30% of a forehand's, with the ball going in precisely because gravity and the downward angle are doing the actual work. A controlled 50 mph overhead wins points at 3.5. A panicked 80 mph swing usually just produces a fault.

There are three overhead situations worth knowing individually. An easy high ball — your opponent's lob came up short — calls for the standard version: full extension, full Sane Smash, aimed cross-court. A deep ball landing behind you calls for turning and shuffling back so it's in front of you again — don't ever try to hit an overhead with the ball behind your body; that's a genuine backhand smash, and at this level the only backhand smash worth attempting is a simple controlled block. If it's too deep to reach comfortably, the easier solution is just letting it bounce — now it's a normal groundstroke. And a ball you're still moving backward to reach calls for planting your feet first, then hitting — never hit an overhead on the run; the Sane Smash is fundamentally a still shot, not a moving one.

At 50+, the Sane Smash is genuinely your reward for hitting good defensive lobs earlier in the point. The ball comes to you, gravity does the heavy lifting, your shoulder never takes a real load. The one risk worth flagging is the head-still cue specifically — your neck really doesn't love craning backward. Keep the chin level, eyes tracking the ball, and let the ball come down to you rather than reaching up for it. If a lob's so high you'd have to crane your neck to track it, just let it bounce. A controlled 3.5-level forehand off the bounce beats an overstretched 50+ overhead every time.

## The Specialty Shots — Lob Volley and Counter-Overhead

These two round out your vertical toolkit. Both are situational rather than everyday shots, but when the moment calls for them, they're genuinely devastating.

**The lob volley is your net emergency parachute.** Use it when you're at net, your opponent's at net or approaching, and you simply can't get a passing shot by them — it's the "I can't get past you, so I'll go over you instead" answer. Grip: Continental, same as the defensive lob. Contact needs to be genuinely high, at or above shoulder height — this shot only works from up there; catch it at waist height or lower and hit a normal volley or half-volley instead, because a lob volley from a low contact point is a near-guaranteed net error. The racket face stays open, almost vertical, and your non-dominant hand reaches up to help track and balance the shot. Aim deep, same target as your regular defensive lob. And critically, recover forward to the net afterward — don't retreat.

It's worth being clear about what this shot actually is: not a winner, but a parachute. It doesn't end the point; it gives you a soft landing when the situation's genuinely dangerous. Use it to escape a spot where you can't pass, then look to attack again on the very next shot. A lob volley followed by a smart net approach is a complete pattern in itself, not just an emergency measure.

**The counter-overhead is your net finishing move.** Use it when you're at net, your opponent lobs over you, and you actually have time to set up. This is the exact same Sane Smash from the previous section, just starting from a net position instead of the baseline — the mechanics are identical. The only real difference is time: you're closer to the net, so the ball has less distance to travel, which means you need to start tracking earlier than you would from the baseline. As soon as the lob leaves your opponent's racket, side-shuffle back — don't stand flat-footed watching it climb. Get into position before it peaks, turn into the same serve-ready stance as the baseline overhead, and run the Sane Smash exactly as described above.

The trap here: many players see the lob, panic, and sprint straight backward without ever turning their body. They arrive looking straight up, off balance, and produce a weak floater. The fix is simply tracking the lob, side-shuffling back, and then genuinely turning into the serve stance — that turn is what gives you the same clean mechanics you already trust on your serve.

There's a genuinely useful doubles pattern worth knowing here too. The vertical game is arguably the best weapon available to 50+ doubles players, because opponents at net are usually only eight to ten feet away from you — a defensive lob is the easiest way to neutralize them entirely, forcing a high overhead on the move. Your partner can then poach the weak reply that often results. The full pattern: your partner serves, you hit a deep defensive lob, your partner poaches the reply. Three shots, one point — a genuine cheat code for 50+ doubles.

## Five Drills to Build the Habit This Week

**The "scratch your back" lob air swing, solo, two minutes a day.** Stand in your open stance, racket in Continental grip, and do the backswing, sweeping low to high with the face slightly open and a shoulder shrug at the top. Twenty air swings a day, every day, for two weeks. This works because the backswing is exactly the part 3.5 players skip — they go straight from ready position to hitting, when the backswing genuinely *is* the shot; without it, there's simply no arc.

**The Sane Smash trophy position, solo, three minutes a day.** Racket in Continental grip, ball in your non-dominant hand, both arms lifted into the trophy position — non-dominant arm pointing at an imaginary ball, racket arm bent with the head above. Hold ten seconds, ten reps. This works because the trophy position is identical between the serve and the overhead — if you can hold it for one, you can hold it for the other, and the drill just builds the body memory directly.

**The Sane Smash from a tossed ball, solo or with a partner, ten minutes.** Stand at the baseline in serve stance while a partner stands at the service line and tosses the ball high toward your forehand overhead position. Track, position, turn, Sane Smash. Goal: ten in a row landing in the correct service box. The cue worth repeating throughout: if the racket feels like it's "swinging" through the ball, you're trying too hard — the motion is a controlled fall, not a swing.

**The defensive lob rally, with a partner, ten minutes.** Start at the baseline together. You feed an easy ball to your partner, they hit a deep groundstroke back, you respond with a defensive slice lob, they let it bounce and return it, you lob again. Goal: five consecutive exchanges without an error. Diagnostic: a lob landing in the service box means your arc's too low or your aim too short — add more shoulder shrug. A lob landing beyond the baseline means you're overhitting — pull back on the shoulder shrug and let the backswing itself do the work instead.

**The lob volley triangle, with a partner, five minutes.** Set up a rough triangle — your partner at the baseline, you at net on the ad side, with cones or just imagination marking the deuce corner of the service box. Your partner feeds a ball at your feet or body; you hit a lob volley to the deuce corner. Ten reps, then switch sides. The check to run: if your lob volley keeps going into the net, you're catching the ball too low — let it rise closer to shoulder height first. The lob volley needs high contact, never low.

## The 50+ Reality Check

A few honest notes on where the wear shows up, and what to do about each one. Shoulder strain on the overhead usually comes from cranking through a high ball instead of letting gravity work — the Sane Smash uses gravity, not muscle, so keep grip pressure around 3-4 out of 10 and reach without cranking. Neck strain comes from craning backward to track a high ball — keep the head still, eyes tracking, let the ball come down to you, and let it bounce if it's simply too high. Knee strain shows up on lob recovery, from sprinting back into position after every lob — use a small-step recovery instead; the lob buys you time specifically so you can walk back, not sprint. Back strain on the overhead usually comes from hyperextending the lower back to reach a high ball — bend the knees, not the back; the trophy position should come from knee bend and hip hinge, not a back arch. Wrist and elbow strain on the offensive lob comes from brushing too hard through the topspin — honestly, just skip the offensive lob entirely at 50+; the defensive slice lob does the same job with zero real strain. A loss of confidence on high balls, showing up as weak, floating "moon" returns, gets rebuilt directly through the tossed-ball Sane Smash drill — ten clean reps in a row and the fear tends to genuinely fade. And playing horizontal tennis only, never reaching for the vertical game, just means points keep getting decided by groundstroke exchanges alone — lead with the defensive lob once a session, and within a month it'll start to feel like a reflex rather than a decision.

If there's one rule above the rest here: you're not trying to win the point with the lob or the overhead. You're trying to not lose it. A defensive lob that lands deep, even if your opponent retrieves it, buys you real recovery time. A Sane Smash that lands in the right service box, even if it comes back, puts you in an attacking position regardless. Survival first, placement second, pace dead last — always, at this level.

A few more tai chi principles worth carrying with you. *Thường Tùng* — always loose — describes the Sane Smash precisely: a relaxed grip, a relaxed shoulder, the racket falling rather than the arm swinging. And *Tụ Bộ* — the dropping step — captures the whole feeling of the overhead: the body's weight drops into the shot rather than the arm swinging through it. The body drops, and the ball drops with it.

The lob and the overhead aren't two separate shots. They're two answers to the same problem: the ball is in the air. See them as a pair, and you start playing the vertical game — and the vertical game is what decides points at this level.
