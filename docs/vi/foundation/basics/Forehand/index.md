# Cú Vung Góc L Của Forehand & Tăng Tốc Muộn

The forehand is the working shot. For most recreational players, it's where the majority of points are won. A reliable, repeatable forehand with depth and topspin is the foundation of every competitive game.

This section covers the modern forehand from the ground up. The main article introduces the L-angle and late acceleration — the two principles that make the modern forehand different from the flat forehand of the 1980s. The companion articles cover the unit turn, the racket head position, the contact point, the Kinh (kinetic chain), and the specific body positions that produce consistent ball-striking.

Read the main article first, then the unit turn and racket head position piece — those two together will change your forehand more than any other reading you do this month. The deeper articles on racket embodiment and the Kinh-Vai-Thuc-Hu axis are for when you're ready to go further.

Practice the unit turn in slow motion for a week before adding pace. Most recreational forehand errors are unit-turn errors. The body knows what to do once you show it the load.

When the forehand starts to leak — usually to the slice or the short ball — come back to the L-angle article. Nine times out of ten, the fix is in the contact point, not the swing.
