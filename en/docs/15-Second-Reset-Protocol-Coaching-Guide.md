# The 15-Second Reset Protocol

Here's a structured tool I want you to actually build into your game, not just understand intellectually: a three-phase routine, run between every single point, that quiets your amygdala, restores your calm, and pulls your trained motor patterns back online before you need them. Think of it as the precision version of a between-point ritual — 15 seconds, three phases, each doing a specific job.

## Phase 1: Physical Release — 0 to 5 Seconds

The point ends. Take one diaphragmatic breath, and let it flush the cortisol and quiet your amygdala. Break your visual lock on whatever just happened — the missed shot, the net, your opponent — and look somewhere neutral instead. That single shift interrupts your amygdala's ongoing threat-feed. A small physical gesture here — adjusting your strings, using your towel — works like a shutdown signal for your inner critic.

## Phase 2: Center and Breathe — 5 to 10 Seconds

Diaphragmatic breathing stimulates your vagus nerve, which activates your parasympathetic brake — mechanically lowering your heart rate and restoring your heart rate variability, the physiological marker of a nervous system that's out of fight-or-flight. Be specific about the rhythm: roughly 4 seconds in, 6 seconds out. Bring your attention down to your center of mass, low in your abdomen — that's your target for this phase, and it counteracts the upward tension your amygdala tends to create.

## Phase 3: Retrieve the Blueprint — 10 to 15 Seconds

Instead of consciously thinking through your next shot — slow, effortful, prefrontal — retrieve a successful memory of the movement instead, a felt sense of correct execution. This isn't tactical planning. It's pulling a pattern from implicit memory. If something broke down technically on the last point, one shadow swing with the correct feeling, right here, tags the right movement back into your body before your next serve.

## Why Exactly 15 Seconds

Your cortisol response to a stressful moment has a short half-life when you actively counter it. Fifteen seconds of structured intervention is enough to break your threat-lock, restore your vagal tone, and re-engage your implicit motor control. Skip this and match pressure creates a feedback loop between anxiety and technical errors — sympathetic arousal stacking, point after point, that eventually tips into full-blown collapse.

## Stability Decay — What You're Actually Preventing

Without a structured reset, your performance erodes progressively across a match — and it's not just physical fatigue doing that. It's accumulated cortisol and unresolved sympathetic arousal, each un-reset point adding to the load. This 15-second protocol is the maintenance routine that keeps that decay from happening in the first place.
