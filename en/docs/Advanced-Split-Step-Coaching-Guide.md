# The Advanced Split-Step

This is the upgraded version of your split-step, where you read your opponent's shot direction while you're still airborne, and land with more weight on the foot opposite the direction you're about to move — setting up an immediately aggressive push toward the ball from a position that's already pre-loaded.

## The Core Mechanism

Your standard split-step lands on both feet at once, weight even, and you react from there. The advanced version has you landing on the counter-foot first — more weight on your left foot if you're about to move right, more weight on your right foot if you're about to move left — using the read you made while still in the air.

Landing on that counter-foot creates a longer push-off runway: the distance between your center of gravity and your planted push-off foot increases, which lets you lean more aggressively and generate more force on that first step. It's the same physics behind any kind of momentum preloading — more distance between your center of gravity and your push-off point means a more forceful launch in that direction.

## The Read Has to Happen in the Air

This only works if you make your read while you're still airborne — before you land, not after. Your anticipatory reading skills need to be developed enough to deliver that directional information during the split-step's airborne phase specifically. Without a reliable read, this move actually works against you — landing on the wrong counter-foot, weighted for a move left when the ball's actually going right, leaves you worse off than a neutral two-footed landing would have.

## The Timing Rule Worth Remembering

Your feet should hit the ground right as you first know which way you need to move. That's the whole principle: the airborne phase of your split-step is your reading window. Your landing resolves that read into a directional lean. And your first push-off follows immediately after you land — no pause, no hesitation.
