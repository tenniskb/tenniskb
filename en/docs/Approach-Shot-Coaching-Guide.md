# The Approach Shot

The approach shot is the groundstroke you hit from inside or near the baseline that starts your move to the net — and its quality directly determines the quality of the net position you get to occupy. A weak approach leaves you volleying from no-man's-land against a clean passing shot. A strong approach produces a high, short reply you can finish comfortably.

Here's the mindset shift worth making: your approach shot was never meant to win the point. It's meant to degrade the quality of the next ball enough that stepping into the net actually becomes safe.

## The Three Qualities That Matter

**Depth** is your highest priority, unless you're specifically hitting a drop shot approach. The deeper your approach lands, the less time your opponent has to set up their passing shot after the bounce.

**Direction** should typically go to your opponent's weaker groundstroke side, and toward whichever side forces the greatest running distance. That does double duty: it produces an off-balance passing shot attempt, and it opens the other side of the court for your finishing volley. A middle-court approach — deep through the center — works differently: it limits your opponent's available angles almost entirely, and the ball comes back to you at net far more reliably.

**Pace and spin balance** is where the heavy approach earns its name: a high-RPM groundstroke driven deep, typically to the backhand corner, with enough pace and spin that your opponent's reply is forced short and defensive. A heavy approach landing within a yard of the baseline and kicking up above shoulder height produces exactly the combination you want — short and high, the ideal setup for a finishing volley. Aim for roughly 70-80% pace here, prioritizing depth and spin over raw velocity. This is a quality-degradation shot, not a winner attempt.

## Three Types, Three Situations

**Heavy topspin approach** is the modern standard on baseline-dominant tours — high RPM, deep to the backhand corner, forcing a defensive reply. It's most effective when you actually have time and a comfortable contact height.

**Slice approach** is your answer when time is limited. The ball stays low, forcing your opponent to hit up on their passing shot, and the slower pace buys you more time to close in and establish good position at net. It's also your natural choice on low balls.

**Drop shot approach** is less conventional but genuinely high-reward. A well-placed drop shot forces your opponent to sprint forward, leaving the opposite side of the court wide open for your volley. It takes real confidence in your touch and real awareness of the surface — far more effective on slow clay where the ball dies, riskier on fast hard courts where it can sit up.

## Your Footwork Changes With the Ball

Off a high ball with time to set up, use the same stance you'd use for any baseline groundstroke — semi-open or neutral. Off a lower, shorter ball, your front leg steps forward, plants, and may hop through your follow-through, carrying your momentum toward the net.

The slice approach specifically uses a glide footwork pattern — feet close to the ground, body low and balanced. On the backhand slice approach, the carioca step is common: your back foot crosses behind and then in front of your lead foot during the swing, letting you get a full body turn while staying balanced through contact.

The principle worth internalizing: measure your steps as you move forward, and use the same stance on your approach that you'd use for a comparable groundstroke from the baseline. Players who tense up on approach shots lose exactly the balance and hip alignment a strong shot depends on.

## Where You End Up After

Once you've hit your approach, move forward quickly, and favor the side you hit it toward. If your approach went toward the deuce singles line, for instance, split a couple feet left of the center line — that's the midpoint between the down-the-line and cross-court passing angles your opponent actually has available.

And always split-step right before your opponent strikes their passing shot attempt — reading their body alignment gives you the anticipatory read you need to move the right direction.
