# Choking

I want to take the word "choking" away from you as an insult and give it back to you as a mechanism — because that's what it actually is. Choking happens when your conscious mind tries to micromanage a stroke that your body already knows how to hit automatically. It's not a character flaw. It's a predictable, mechanical consequence of conscious interference, and once you see it that way, you can actually do something about it.

## What's Actually Happening

Under normal conditions, your stroke runs on motor programs you've built through thousands of repetitions — programs that operate far faster than conscious thought ever could. The moment pressure, fear, or over-thinking pulls your conscious mind into that process, it starts sending conflicting signals into a system that needs seamless sequencing. And the results are entirely predictable: opposing muscles start co-contracting instead of firing in sequence, tension builds across your whole kinetic chain, your arm short-circuits the rest of your body, your racket head speed collapses, and the shot ends up lacking exactly the power and precision your conscious mind was trying so hard to protect.

## Choke vs. Flow — One Variable

Here's the single distinction that matters: where is the effort coming from? When you choke, effort comes from your conscious mind forcing an outcome — strain, over-steering, muscles bracing against themselves. When you're in flow, effort comes from your trained body simply running its program — lightness, rhythm, an elastic release. Same body, same skill level, completely different result, and the difference is entirely about who's driving.

## What Triggers It

High-pressure points — break points, set points, match points — are the obvious ones. But so is fear of failure, where losing quietly gets equated with your own worth. So is the judgment spiral: you miss a shot, you criticize yourself, that criticism spikes anxiety, anxiety produces bracing, bracing produces the next miss. And so is what I'd call the drift — losing your between-point structure and letting your mind ruminate freely between points instead of resetting.

## How You Prevent It

Everything that keeps your conscious mind in its proper, supporting role helps here. A between-point ritual resets your system before every single point. Non-judgmental observation stops the judgment spiral before it can escalate. A simple bounce-hit cue anchors your attention in present, sensory data instead of outcome-worry. Reframing your fear of failure removes the sense of threat that triggers your amygdala in the first place. And a quiet, present-moment mental state — what some traditions call mushin — bypasses that threat response at the neural level entirely.
