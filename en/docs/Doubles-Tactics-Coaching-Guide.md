# Doubles Tactics

Doubles runs on a completely different engine than singles. The court's wider — 36 feet instead of 27 — but four players on it shrinks the actual openings you have to work with. Strategy shifts away from baseline grinding and toward net dominance and genuinely synchronized teamwork. Here's the single fact worth building your whole doubles game around: the team that occupies the net first wins roughly 70-80% of points at a competitive level.

## Pillar One: The Race to the Net

Every doubles point has one primary objective — both players reaching the attacking zone, inside the service line, before or immediately after the first shot exchange. Two players at net create a real wall: four rackets in the forward position mathematically eliminate most of your opponents' passing angles.

Serve-and-volley remains the gold standard in doubles specifically. Moving forward immediately after your serve lets you join your partner at net, completing that wall, and forces your returner into hitting a winner from a defensive, time-compressed position.

The chip-and-charge is your return-of-serve version of the same idea: a low, short-angled slice return — the chip — forces your opponents to volley upward. You charge the net on the same motion, arriving in the attacking zone in time to volley their upward reply back down at a steep angle.

## Pillar Two: The Center Pipe

The single most consistently effective shot in doubles is hitting straight down the middle between your two opponents — what I call the center pipe. It creates two effects at once.

First, confusion and hesitation: a ball down the middle forces a split-second delay while your opponents figure out who's actually taking the shot. That timing disruption breaks their point construction rhythm before they've even swung. Second, angle elimination: exactly like center theory in singles, a ball down the middle removes their ability to hit a sharp-angled winner — they're forced to hit back through the center of the net, directly into the strength of your team's position.

The center pipe is particularly brutal against a team where one partner has a dominant forehand. Forcing the ball to the boundary between their respective forehands creates a communication problem that neither player's individual strength can solve alone.

## Pillar Three: The Active Poach

The net player — whoever's not serving or returning — is the most dangerous player on the court, but only if they're genuinely active rather than static. A static net player is just a passenger. An active one is a threat that distorts every shot your opponent tries to hit.

Your lateral split step has to happen the instant your opponent makes contact — not once the ball's already in flight. That's what keeps you in reactive readiness for either the poach or holding your position.

The intercept — the actual poach — means moving diagonally forward to cut off a cross-court return. That puts real pressure on the other team's conscious decision-making, forcing them into higher-risk down-the-alley attempts instead of their comfortable cross-court pattern. And here's the part worth remembering: even if you never actually move, the mere threat of the poach degrades the quality of your opponents' cross-court returns, because their visual system has already registered you as a threat.

## Two Advanced Formations Worth Knowing

The I-formation has your partner crouch low right on the center service line before the serve, creating genuine uncertainty for the returner about which direction the net player's about to move. That's a real psychological weapon: the returner can't fully commit to cross-court or down-the-line without risking interception — a high-pressure decision they have to make in under 150 milliseconds. To execute it, the server signals pre-serve, hand hidden behind the back, which direction the net player will move, and covers whichever side gets vacated. This works best against strong cross-court returners who've already neutralized the standard poach with consistent low returns.

The Australian formation has both of you standing on the same side of the court before the serve, forcing your returner into a low-percentage down-the-line shot just to avoid the net player. It works best against returners who hit almost exclusively cross-court — though it does leave the server's backhand side temporarily exposed if the return sneaks in behind them.

## Doubles vs. Singles at a Glance

| Dimension | Singles | Doubles |
|---|---|---|
| Primary power axis | Baseline groundstrokes | Net position |
| Court geometry | Wide angles available | Narrower lanes; four-player coverage |
| Key shot | Deep cross-court groundstroke | Low chip to the feet; center pipe |
| First-strike weapon | Serve plus first ball | Serve-and-volley / chip-and-charge |
| Primary metric | Rally consistency | Net occupation rate |
| Percentage-tennis adaptation | 70% cross-court | 70% center pipe plus net transition |

## Your Communication System

At a competitive level, doubles genuinely requires a pre-point signal system. The net player signals the server with a hidden hand behind their back:

| Signal | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Open palm | I'm staying — server covers their own side |
| Closed fist | I'm poaching — server crosses behind to cover |
| One finger | I-formation, I'm going right |
| Two fingers | I-formation, I'm going left |

Without signals, your server has no idea whether to cover for a poach, and your team ends up operating as two individuals instead of one unit. Communication is the actual multiplier that makes a doubles team greater than the sum of two singles players.
