# Drop Volley

The drop volley — sometimes called the touch volley at its most extreme — is a net shot where you deliberately absorb the incoming ball's pace so it dies just over the net with almost no forward velocity. A standard volley reflects and redirects the ball's energy. This one kills it. Mechanically, it's the opposite of a normal volley, and it's one of the purest expressions of absorption in tennis.

## The Core Mechanic: Retraction, Not Redirection

To pull this off, your racket has to do the opposite of a standard redirection — it retreats slightly at the moment of impact instead of holding firm or pushing forward. Think velvet glove, not wooden board: your string bed gives way and cradles the ball rather than propelling it. Grip pressure sits around a 4 out of 10 — what I'd call a cradle pulse rather than a squeeze.

## Getting the Face Angle Right

For the lob-drop variant — redirecting the ball upward and short — your face needs to be open around 40-45 degrees. That converts the ball's incoming horizontal energy into vertical energy instead: it goes up briefly, then dies. That soft pulse at around 4/10 is what keeps the ball from flying past the baseline — your hand is acting as a shock absorber, killing pace as it redirects the ball upward.

## Where It Sits on the Pressure Spectrum

| Shot | Pressure | Intent |
|---|---|---|
| Termination Volley | 9/10 | Heavy ball skids deep |
| Neutral Volley | 6/10 | Safe depth, absorbs some pace |
| **Drop Volley** | **2-4/10** | **Kills pace; ball drops short** |

This shot sits at the extreme soft end of the grip pressure spectrum — the feather, where maximum softness at impact produces the maximum effect.

## Its Cousin on the Baseline

The drop volley shares its core mechanism with the dead-hand finish groundstroke drop shot: both involve a real drop in grip pressure right at contact, both rely on the strings absorbing pace rather than reflecting it, and both produce backspin that keeps the ball low and short after it bounces. The difference is just context — the dead-hand finish is a disguised groundstroke; the drop volley is the same idea executed at net with far less time to work with.

## Disguise Is Everything

A well-executed drop volley looks identical to a regular block volley right up until the instant of contact. You maintain that disguise by keeping your wrist structure intact until the moment of retraction, keeping the exact same ready position and approach you'd use for any other volley, and only reducing grip pressure and allowing retraction at the very last moment — no visible softening before the ball actually arrives.

## Where It Goes Wrong

If your grip pressure's too high, the strings reflect instead of absorb and the ball flies past the baseline. If you retract too much, the face collapses and the ball goes into the net. If your grip softens before the ball arrives, your disguise is gone and your opponent reads it and passes you. And if you stand up before the ball's actually left the strings, it gets pulled straight into the net.
