# Forehand Stance Selection

You've got a split second, right after reading the incoming ball's speed, height, and landing zone, to choose which of three forehand stances to use — open, semi-open, or neutral. Each one produces a genuinely different balance of linear and rotational power through your contact zone, and picking the right one matters more than most players realize.

## Open Stance

Feet parallel to the baseline, wider than your shoulders, knees bent. Your outside leg — right leg for a right-hander — loads most of the energy here. The coiling between your hips and shoulders generates the primary torque, and your push-off comes from that outside leg, up and forward.

Use this on wide, fast, or high balls where you don't have time to step through, on balls hit to the middle of the court at waist height or above, and anytime you need immediate recovery — the open stance's rotational load-and-push-back is inherently the fastest of the three to reset from.

Your power here comes almost entirely from angular momentum — rotational torque from your hip-shoulder stretch and your hip swivel. And the recovery advantage is real: because your outside leg loads and pushes back in one motion, getting back to center needs no untangling steps — your legs are already positioned to push in either direction.

## Semi-Open Stance

Feet staggered at roughly 45 degrees, left foot in front of right. Your weight loads more evenly between both legs than in the open stance, with the push coming primarily from your lead leg, up and forward into the shot.

Use this on inside-out and inside-in forehands, on balls above waist height hit toward the middle of the court, and whenever you have enough preparation time and maximum power is the priority. Your power here is a genuine combination — angular momentum from your hip-shoulder coil, plus linear momentum from that staggered foot position giving you more forward weight than the open stance provides.

Here's the hierarchy worth remembering: if time allows, use semi-open rather than open — your lead foot position gives you greater forward weight distribution, and that means a heavier contact.

## Neutral (Closed) Stance

Step directly forward with your front foot toward the net — less hip rotation available here. Your weight transfer is clearly linear, mass moving forward through the contact point.

Use this on short balls that need a real drive through the court, on approach shots where forward momentum into the net is intentional, and on low balls in the middle of the court. Your power source is primarily linear momentum — the step-through carries your body mass forward into the ball.

The real trade-off: this is the slowest to recover from of the three. The cross-body step needs untangling before you can move laterally again, so reserve it for situations where a forward approach naturally follows.

## Your Feet Need to Stay Free

On any open or semi-open forehand, your feet can't be locked down. As your shoulders rotate aggressively through the shot, your feet need to pivot and lift to allow the full rotation — your left foot finishing pointed toward your target, your right foot resting lightly on its toe, heel off the ground. Lock your feet in place and you interrupt that rotational flow, putting real torsional stress on your knees as your shoulders counter-rotate against hips that can't move.

## Matching Stance to Ball Height

| Ball Height | Preferred Stance | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Above the shoulder | Open or semi-open | Rotational torque generates lift; wide base handles a high ball |
| Waist height | Semi-open | Best balance of power and control |
| Below the waist | Neutral or semi-open | Forward weight transfer keeps contact in front |
| Very low, defensive | Open, wider base | Lowers your center of gravity; wide base prevents collapsing |
