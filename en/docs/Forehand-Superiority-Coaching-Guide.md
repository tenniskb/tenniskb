# Forehand Superiority

There's a documented physical and psychological advantage the forehand has over the backhand, and it holds true at both the professional and recreational level. It's worth understanding, because it explains a lot of what you already see in how players — including yourself — actually play under pressure.

## The Physical Case

For most players, at every skill level, the forehand is simply the stronger stroke. Its mechanics allow for more involvement from your dominant arm and shoulder through the kinetic chain, a more comfortable range for your contact point, better reach on wide balls, and more natural power generation and timing overall.

There's a real psychological asymmetry that follows from this mechanical reality: when a ball arrives on your forehand side, your mindset typically shifts toward offense. When the same ball arrives on your backhand side, your offensive expectations quietly lower — even if your technique is solid on both sides. That shift in mindset isn't just in your head; it's tracking a real mechanical difference.

## What Professional Play Shows Us

The tour's own history reflects this. The inside forehand — running around your backhand to hit a forehand instead — became one of the most devastating baseline weapons in the game, developed systematically by Nick Bollettieri with Jim Courier back in the late 1980s. Courier's run to world number one was built partly on a game plan designed specifically to maximize forehand use and minimize backhand exposure. And the inside-out forehand has gone from being a specialist shot to a completely standard professional weapon.

The fact that top players routinely run around their own backhand — accepting worse court position in exchange for hitting a forehand instead — is direct evidence of just how real this asymmetry is. They're making a calculated trade: worse positioning for a stronger shot, and the math works out in their favor often enough that it's become standard practice.
