# Grip Anchor — Bevel 2 and Base Knuckle

Let's get specific about your Continental grip, because "hold it like a hammer" only gets you so far. The real anchor point is the base knuckle of your index finger, sitting right on the corner of bevel 2. That one small placement decides almost everything else about how your volley behaves.

## What This One Placement Actually Does

When that base knuckle sits on bevel 2, your palm ends up slightly behind and under the handle, and two things follow from that. First, your racket face naturally opens to around 15 degrees at rest — exactly the starting angle you want for underspin and touch. Second, your index finger becomes a structural pillar against the ball's impact — it's literally resisting the ball's momentum for you. Let that knuckle slide up toward bevel 1, and you lose that resistance; the racket head "gates" backward on contact instead of holding firm.

## Your Knuckle Is Also a Sensor

Here's something worth paying attention to: that base knuckle isn't just structural, it's where you feel your grip. Sitting right at the junction of your index finger and the handle, it's constantly feeding you information — is the face open or closed, has your grip drifted under pressure, is your intent actually matching what the racket's about to do. Get that knuckle properly anchored and you'll be able to feel your face angle without ever looking down at the strings. That's the difference between touch that's actually controlled and touch that's just lucky.

## The Trigger Finger Refinement

Once the base knuckle is anchored, there's one more adjustment worth making: don't bunch your fingers together in a tight fist. Spread your index finger slightly higher up the handle, leaving about an inch of gap to your middle finger. That gap does two jobs — it braces the racket against twisting when a heavy shot catches the frame's edge, and it gives you a lever for tiny, precise adjustments to your face angle right in the strike window.

## Two-Handed Backhand: The Same Anchor, New Job

If you're playing two hands, your bottom hand in Continental grip becomes your stability anchor — keeping your wrist angle neutral so the top hand can drive the power without the whole structure wobbling. Watch your dominant wrist during the backswing; it shouldn't drift more than about 10 degrees if you want to hold that anchor. And the same grip-pressure rhythm applies here too — around a 3 out of 10 in ready position, spiking briefly to 8 only in that split-second of contact.

## What Grip Drift Looks Like, and How to Catch It

Under match pressure, your base knuckle can quietly migrate away from bevel 2 without you noticing — you can't exactly check your grip mid-point. The fix is simple: keep your non-dominant hand on the throat of the racket during your transition. That contact gives you a tactile check that your hitting hand hasn't drifted before your split step.
