# Grip Pressure

I want you to think of your hand as a dial, not a switch. Most players treat grip pressure as on or off — squeeze or don't — but at the net, grip pressure is one of the most powerful tactical tools you have, because it decides how much of the incoming ball's pace you absorb versus send straight back. The rule I want stuck in your head: firm enough to guide, soft enough to absorb.

## Three Pressures, Three Outcomes

There isn't one "correct" grip pressure for volleys — there are three, and you pick depending on what you're trying to do.

When you want to put the ball away, squeeze hard — call it a 9 out of 10. That's your termination volley. The ball comes off heavy and skids deep, because you're reflecting almost all its pace back.

When you're just staying in the point, hold around a 6. That's your neutral block — you're absorbing some pace, but still sending the ball back with depth and safety.

When you want touch — a drop volley, something that dies just over the net — go down to a 2. Barely holding on. You're not guiding the ball anymore so much as letting your strings swallow its energy so it has nothing left when it lands.

## Why This Works: The Strings Do the Absorbing

Here's the physics behind it, because I think it'll change how you feel this. Your string bed is an energy-management system between the ball and your arm. Squeeze hard, and the strings are locked rigid — there's nowhere for the ball's energy to go except straight into your arm. On a 100 mph passing shot with a death grip, all of that force drives into the tendons on the outside of your elbow. That's not a hypothetical — that's the direct mechanism behind sudden-onset tennis elbow.

Loosen your grip, and the strings can flex and deflect on contact, soaking up velocity before it reaches your arm. On a half-volley, you actually want a kind of split: your wrist and arm structure stay firm — you need that shape to hold — but your grip itself stays soft enough that the strings can still absorb what's left of the ball's speed. I call that the soft-hand illusion: firm frame, soft grip, at the same time.

The drop volley takes this even further. You're not just absorbing — you're actually letting the racket retreat slightly on contact, like a cradle catching a fall rather than a wall stopping one. That's closer to a 4 out of 10, not a hard pulse at all.

## What Happens If You Get This Wrong

A death grip — 10 out of 10, every time, on every ball — feels like control, but it's the opposite. It removes all of your string bed's ability to help you, and it puts every ounce of a heavy ball's force straight into your tendons. Over time, or sometimes in a single ugly moment against a hard-hit ball, that's how you end up with tennis elbow, or worse, valgus overload on the inside of the elbow.

There's also a subtler cost: squeezing hard triggers your whole arm to tense up and take over consciously, which actually disrupts the natural, automatic sequencing your body would otherwise use to generate power efficiently. A moderate squeeze lets your hand do its real job — the last link in the chain, not the one doing all the work.
