# Grip Pulse

Here's the thing I want you to unlearn first: forget the word "punch." Every volley cue that tells you to "punch the ball" is quietly wrecking your technique, and I want to replace it with something that actually matches what your hand should do at contact — the grip pulse.

Watch a good volleyer up close and you won't see a punch at all. The arm arrives at the ball and stops. It doesn't drive forward, it doesn't follow through into the court. What actually happens is a split-second squeeze of the hand, right at contact, and then it's gone.

## What's Really Happening

Through your unit turn, keep the grip loose — barely holding on. That looseness is what lets your chest and shoulders do the turning instead of your hand fighting for control. Then, right at the instant the ball hits the strings, every finger tightens at once, hard, for about as long as the ball is actually on your strings — somewhere in the 30-50 millisecond range. That's it. That's the whole pulse.

What that brief squeeze does: it decelerates the racket head right as the ball arrives, locks your wrist angle in place, and gives the ball something solid to redirect off of. Then you let go again and you're already back in ready position.

## Why "Punch" Ruins This

Say the word "punch" to yourself and your body wants to extend forward — that's what punches do. On a volley, that's exactly the wrong instinct:

- Your arm keeps traveling past the contact point instead of stopping there
- That forward momentum throws off your balance
- You're still finishing the "punch" when the next ball is already coming, so recovery is late
- Worse, the extension usually breaks the laid-back wrist position you need, and the racket head drops right when you need it steady

Cut the word "punch" from your vocabulary and give your hand a grip pulse instead, and most of that disappears on its own.

## How This Fits With Everything Else

Think of the volley as three connected pieces. Your chest turns the racket to the ball — that's the still-wall part. The grip pulse is the brief, firm moment of actual contact. Then you recover immediately back to a wide, ready base. Skip the pulse and the whole structure goes soft right when it matters — the wall gives way at the exact moment it's supposed to hold.

## Why Continental Grip Makes This Easier

If you're volleying with a Continental grip — heel of the palm on bevel 2 — you're in the best possible position to execute this pulse. It's the same grip for forehand and backhand volleys, so there's no scrambling to change grips mid-point. And the wrist naturally sits in that laid-back position the pulse depends on, far more easily than it would in an Eastern grip.
