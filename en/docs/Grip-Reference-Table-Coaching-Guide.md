# Grip Reference Table

Rod Laver had it right: the grip determines everything. Here's the reference I want you to actually internalize — not memorize, internalize — for the four grips you'll actually use, and when each one earns its place.

## The Bevel System, Quickly

Your grip is defined by which numbered bevel the heel of your palm rests on — numbered 1 through 8 clockwise for a right-hander, starting from the top flat edge.

| Grip | Bevel | Main Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| Continental | 2 | Best for slices, volleys, serves — the universal grip |
| Eastern | 3 | Classic flat hitting; versatile, easy to learn |
| Semi-Western | 4 | Modern standard; balances power and heavy topspin |
| Western | 5 | Maximum topspin; built for high-bouncing balls |

## What Each Grip Actually Gives You

**Continental** is your handshake grip — face perpendicular to the ground with a neutral wrist, which is exactly why it's natural for slices, volleys, and serves, where you want a relatively open face at contact. It's the universal grip for net play specifically because it lets you hold your shape on both forehand and backhand volleys without ever changing your hand position — and at net, you genuinely don't have time to change grips between shots. Its one real weakness: on topspin groundstrokes, that same open face sends the ball long or floating unless you compensate with real wrist snap.

**Eastern** is your flat-hitting grip — more closed than Continental, giving you a naturally forward face on the forehand. This is your grip for flat, penetrating drives, and it's a good starting point if you're still learning topspin or you prefer a lower contact point. Federer built his whole game around a strong Eastern grip — it's what gave him that efficient, linear, penetrating ball flight. It's not suited to the extreme topspin the modern baseline game demands, but nothing produces a cleaner flat drive.

**Semi-Western** is the modern standard for a reason — moving your hand further under the handle closes your face naturally at contact, producing topspin without needing to compensate with your wrist. Reach for this on mid-court balls that need to dip in with pace, on heavy topspin cross-courts, and on inside-out forehands from an open stance. Watch elite players closely and you'll notice a small bevel shift mid-unit-turn — the off-hand holds the throat while the hitting hand quietly micro-adjusts to the right bevel before the swing even starts.

**Western** gets you maximum topspin — palm fully under the handle, face strongly closed, requiring a steep low-to-high path to generate any forward momentum. This is your grip for high-bouncing balls where you need that steep path to reach contact above shoulder height, for extreme topspin on clay, and for defensive topspin lobs from wide. Its weakness is real: low balls that need a flat or open face become extremely difficult, and without real wrist compensation, that closed face just puts the ball in the net.

## Choosing on the Fly

| Situation | Grip | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Pulled wide, low ball, defensive | Continental or Eastern | Lets you chip or slice back effectively |
| Mid-court, need pace and dip | Semi-Western | Angular momentum plus wrist snap |
| High ball, aggressive topspin | Semi-Western or Western | Steep swing path is naturally suited |
| Any volley | Continental | Universal — no change needed forehand to backhand |
| Serve | Continental | Cleanest pronation pathway |
