# The Half-Volley

I want to change how you think about the half-volley — striking the ball the instant after it bounces at your feet. It used to be treated as a desperation save, proof you were caught out of position. Not anymore. Against the kind of heavy, dipping topspin players hit today, you'll get caught with the ball landing at your shoelaces before you can reach it in the air constantly — and mastering this shot isn't optional anymore, it's mandatory for surviving at net.

## Where the Power Actually Comes From

Unlike an air volley, which redirects linear force, the half-volley redirects rebound force. The ball already has maximum energy from its bounce. Your racket's job is to act as a damped wall — using minimal forward push to reflect that energy back toward the baseline.

Here's the rule that matters most: don't swing at a half-volley. Adding your own power on top of the bounce energy sends the ball long or produces a trajectory you can't control. Your racket face needs to be slightly more closed than a standard low volley, to counteract the ball's natural upward momentum coming off the bounce. This is a reflection, not a drive.

There's a softer version worth knowing too — the feather touch. Loosen your grip down to around 2/10 at impact and you can deaden the bounce energy entirely, dropping the ball short instead. This only works when the incoming ball is under about 50 mph.

## Prepare Before the Bounce, Not After

Because the ball rises so fast off the bounce, any backswing at all puts your contact point late. Your racket needs to be set before the ball even bounces — the exact opposite of every other volley, where preparation follows the trigger. Here, it comes first.

## Getting Your Body Low Enough

This shot demands the most extreme lowering of your center of gravity in all of net play. Get your eyes nearly level with the net cord — dropping your head until your eyes are close to parallel with the bounce point, because looking down at it from height throws off your depth perception by several inches and you'll frame it. Hinge your knees deeply — your back knee should almost touch the court, giving you a tripod-like stability to handle the violent force of a rising ball. And keep your racket face perpendicular, slightly more closed than usual, to manage that upward momentum.

## Where to Send It

When you're forced into a difficult half-volley, redirect it deep down the center of your opponent's court. That takes away their angle for a sharp passing shot on their next ball, buys you roughly 250 milliseconds to close in toward the net, and turns what was a pure survival shot into an actual reset that keeps you in control of the point.

## Common Errors

Bending at your waist instead of your knees opens your racket face and pops the ball up for an easy overhead against you. Adding your own power on top of the bounce sends the ball long — your effort compounds the bounce force instead of redirecting it. And looking up at the net before contact costs you the extra 100 milliseconds you need — keep your eyes on the bounce spot a beat longer than feels natural.
