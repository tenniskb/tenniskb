# The Heavy Approach

The heavy approach is your primary net-entry weapon if you're a baseline-dominant player — a high-RPM topspin groundstroke driven deep into your opponent's court, typically to their backhand corner, with enough pace and spin to force a short, defensive reply. Like every approach shot, it isn't meant to win the point outright. Its entire purpose is degrading the quality of whatever ball comes back to you.

A heavy approach that lands within a yard of the baseline and kicks up above shoulder height produces exactly what you want: a reply that's both short and high — the ideal setup for a finishing volley.

## The Mechanics

Hit this at roughly 70-80% pace, prioritizing depth and spin over flat velocity. Going for maximum pace on an approach shot is a common and costly error — pace without spin actually reduces ball height, handing your opponent a more comfortable contact point and killing the forcing effect a high kick has on jamming their shoulder.

Your target zone: within a yard of the baseline, into the backhand corner. That placement denies your opponent their own plus-one forehand, forces a defensive stroke from the deepest position they could be in, and typically produces exactly the short, high reply you're after.

Closing matters just as much as the approach itself. Move to the net immediately after you hit it, arriving in the kill zone — the service line or closer — before your opponent's defensive ball even crosses the net. A slow close leaves you stuck in no-man's-land, exactly where a low passing shot becomes impossible to handle comfortably.

## Why the Backhand Corner Specifically

Directing your heavy approach cross-court to the backhand corner is the percentage play for four real reasons: it denies your opponent their plus-one forehand — their most powerful weapon; the backhand is typically the weaker passing-shot wing for most players; cross-court is geometrically safer than going down the line; and it sets up a clean geometric bisector — you recover toward center after your approach, and a cross-court passing shot from the backhand corner travels back through that same zone you're now covering.

## Why This Replaced the Flat Drive Approach

The traditional flat-drive approach, popularized in the serve-and-volley era, relied on pace alone to rush your opponent. The heavy approach relies on spin and placement instead. As baseliners got better at redirecting pace, the heavy topspin approach displaced the flat drive as the standard transition shot, for three concrete reasons: it produces a higher bounce, making the defensive reply genuinely harder to execute well; the slower ball gives you more time to actually close the net; and it forces a worse contact point for your opponent's passing shot — above shoulder height instead of a comfortable strike zone.
