# Petit Bras

Petit Bras — French for "short arm" — is what your body looks like from the outside when neural reversion happens under pressure: your racket decelerates through contact, your grip tightens, your kinetic chain shortens down to just your arm, and the shot collapses. It's the visible expression of the whole amygdala hijack sequence playing out in real time.

The name describes the physical result. The actual cause is your brain forcibly handing control back from the basal ganglia — your implicit, automatic system — to the prefrontal cortex, your conscious, deliberate one.

## What Happens

Once your amygdala flips on fight-or-flight, your brain stops trusting its own automated motor programs. The implicit chain that normally fires from the ground up, sequence by sequence, gets interrupted. Your prefrontal cortex tries to consciously steer the ball instead. Your muscles tighten. Your arm decelerates in an attempt to hang onto control. Your wrist locks early, killing the stretch-shortening cycle you need. And your racket face angle ends up being managed consciously instead of delivered automatically the way it should be.

The stroke that results is mechanically recognizable: your already-compact backswing collapses further, contact happens late, pace drops hard, and the ball often floats with no real depth or spin.

## It's Your Brain Protecting You

Here's the reframe worth holding onto: Petit Bras isn't a technical flaw, and it isn't a mental weakness. It's your brain's biological survival mechanism functioning exactly as designed. Your amygdala perceives a threat — match pressure, identity risk, whatever it is — and responds by tightening your muscles and reducing explosive movement to protect you from perceived danger. The cost to your performance is just a trade-off your survival system has no way of knowing isn't worth making.

## Catching It Early

There are recognizable warning signs before full Petit Bras sets in: a death grip, where your pressure exceeds what's actually functional; rapid, uncontrolled eye movement, your visual system switching from predictive to purely reactive; and rising tension in your forearm and shoulder before you've even started your swing.

Your response, once you notice these: run a pre-point ritual that hands control back to your implicit system, use parasympathetic breathing to calm your sympathetic arousal, and build the kind of awareness that catches these first signs before the full collapse takes hold.
