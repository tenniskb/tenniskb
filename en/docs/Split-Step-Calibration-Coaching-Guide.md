# Split-Step Calibration

This is the fine-tuning that separates a real split-step from just hopping in place: the small two-footed hop, timed to land in a perfectly balanced, triple-flexed state at the exact moment directional information becomes available — setting you up for an immediately explosive first step. It's the reset that carries you from one movement into the next, on every single point, whether you're at net or on the baseline.

## What a Good Landing Actually Requires

Your hop needs to land at roughly the moment your opponent makes contact with the ball, and the landing itself needs four things to be true at once. Triple flexion — your ankles, knees, and hips all bending simultaneously, lowering your center of gravity and loading your stretch-shortening cycle. A wide base — feet landing noticeably wider than shoulder-width, which is the only way to lower your center of gravity enough, especially useful for keeping your eyes near net-cord level on an approach volley. Contact on the balls of your feet, not your heels. And directional neutrality — genuine bilateral symmetry, no lean toward either side. Any lean at all closes the door on whichever direction you leaned away from.

## Why Your Feet Hitting the Ground Matters Neurologically

The impact of your feet on the court during this hop does more than position you physically — it resets the proprioceptive sensors in your ankles and knees, signaling your brain that your recovery phase is over and your tracking phase needs to begin. At the very top of your hop, you're briefly weightless — what I'd call the zero-G phase, the instant before gravity starts pulling you back down. Getting your split-step's timing right is what determines whether you can use that free-falling energy for your next move without spending extra muscular effort to get there.

## The 150-Millisecond Target

Elite players keep their ground contact time — from landing to first step, not time spent in the air — under roughly 150-200 milliseconds. Go over that window and the elastic energy your stretch-shortening cycle stored just dissipates as heat, and you're stuck with a slower, purely muscle-driven first step instead. This ground contact time is genuinely one of the most useful things to track if you want an objective read on movement quality.

## Where It Goes Wrong

A flat-foot landing — heels first instead of the balls of your feet — eliminates your ankle flexion, bypasses your stretch-shortening cycle entirely, and costs you roughly 150 milliseconds of pure neurological latency before you can even start your first step. You can hear this one: a loud slap or thud on contact. A directional bias — leaning toward one side, often the backhand — pre-commits you to that direction and kills your explosive response to the other side. Mistiming in either direction costs you too: hop too early and your elastic energy's already dissipated before you even know which way to go; hop too late and you're still airborne when the ball's struck, delaying everything downstream. And a stiff-leg landing — not enough knee bend — eliminates your spring pre-load entirely; you end up falling into your next step instead of exploding into it.

## A Drill Worth Running: The Quiet Landing

Practice your split-step until it produces literally no sound. A silent landing physically requires proper ankle flexion and real triple flexion — your foot has to absorb the impact through elastic tissue rather than bone slamming into the court. This drill self-corrects a flat-foot landing without needing a coach cueing you every single rep.
