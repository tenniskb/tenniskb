# The Split Step

The split step is the small two-footed hop that lands at the exact instant your opponent makes contact with the ball, putting your body into a loaded, ready state — able to react instantly in any direction. It's the first link in every single shot you hit. Without a good split step, your kinetic chain simply can't fire correctly on whatever comes next.

Don't think of this as a "waiting" step. It's a nervous-system trigger that loads your stretch-shortening cycle for your first reactive step.

## Getting the Timing Right

Your split step has to land at the precise moment your opponent contacts the ball — not before, not after. Land too early, and you're stuck waiting until the ball's direction is clear, which costs you your explosive first step. Land too late, and the ball's already traveled too far, forcing you to chase and lose position. Land right, and you get a short amortization phase, your stretch-shortening cycle fires back explosively, and your first step is genuinely fast.

## What's Actually Happening in Your Legs

When you land from a split step, three things happen in sequence. First, an eccentric phase — your legs absorb the landing force, and your tendons and muscles stretch under that load. Second, an amortization phase — this needs to be extremely short, under 100 milliseconds for elite players. Third, a concentric phase — your stretch-shortening cycle fires back, producing an explosive first step. The stiffer your legs are through this sequence, the shorter your amortization phase, and the faster your first step.

## Waking Up Your Senses First

Simple reaction drills with a tennis ball — catch drills — before you start playing wake up your proprioceptors without burning through your explosive energy system. This is a genuinely good nervous-system warm-up before any session that demands quick reactions.

## What Your Body Should Look Like

A wide stance, low center of gravity — a real athletic stance. Knees noticeably bent, never standing tall. Weight on the balls of your feet, not your heels. Arms relaxed and ready.

## Where This Goes Wrong

Bad timing — splitting before your opponent hits — forces you to stand and wait, and you lose your whole stretch-shortening advantage. Skipping the split step entirely means starting from a dead stop, and your first step ends up 30-40% slower. Jumping too high creates a bigger landing impact, a longer amortization phase, and a slower response — it stops functioning as a nervous-system trigger at all. And landing hard, heels first, kills your stretch-shortening cycle and raises your real injury risk at the knee and ankle.
