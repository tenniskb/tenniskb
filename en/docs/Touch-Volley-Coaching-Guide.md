# Touch Volley

The touch volley — sometimes called the feather — is the softest version of the volley you'll hit, executed at a grip pressure of around 2 out of 10. By keeping your hand extremely soft through impact, you dampen the ball's energy instead of reflecting it, letting it drop just over the net rather than traveling deep. It's the most delicate expression of absorption at net, and honestly the best entry point for understanding the whole grip pressure spectrum.

## What Your Hand Is Actually Doing

Grip pressure at 2/10 isn't a weak grip — it's a deliberately soft one, and there's a real difference. Tension in your forearm stays minimal; your hand receives the ball rather than striking it. At that low pressure, your string bed deflects more freely, absorbing a much greater share of the ball's incoming kinetic energy, so less energy returns to the ball and it slows down dramatically. Your wrist and elbow structure stays intact even at that soft grip — that structure is what still directs the ball; the softness is only what kills its pace. And unlike a termination volley, there's no forward racket drive here at all. Your racket meets the ball and does very little — the strings do the actual work.

## The Three Volley Pressures

| Shot | Pressure | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Termination Volley | 9/10 | Heavy, skids deep |
| Neutral Volley | 6/10 | Safe depth, absorbs some pace |
| **Touch Volley** | **2/10** | **Drops short; kills pace** |

The touch volley isn't really a separate shot type — it's the exact same mechanics as a block volley, just executed with radically reduced grip pressure. That's precisely what makes disguising it possible.

## Why It's So Hard to Read

Because the touch volley needs no backswing or special preparation, it's one of the most deceptive shots in the sport. Your opponent sees the same split-step, the same approach, the same arm position they'd see on any other volley — and they can't detect the grip softness until the ball's already dropping short. Keeping your wrist structure intact throughout is what makes this disguise possible: the instant your wrist visibly softens, the shot is telegraphed.

## Its Relationship to the Drop Volley

|  | Touch Volley | Drop Volley |
|---|---|---|
| Grip Pressure | 2/10 | 2-4/10 |
| Racket Motion | Neutral, no retraction | Slight retraction at impact |
| Racket Face | Standard volley angle | 40-45° open, for the lob variant |
| Ball Trajectory | Drops short, forward | Pops up slightly, then dies |

The drop volley adds active retraction on top of the touch volley's passive softness — think of the touch volley as the foundational skill underneath it.

## The Same Principle at the Half-Volley

The touch principle carries over to the half-volley too: keeping a soft grip, around 5/10, while your arm stays structurally rigid creates what I'd call the soft-hand illusion — your strings absorb enough of the ball's velocity to keep it from firing too deep, while your wrist and elbow keep directional control. The safe target here is short and angled cross-court, forcing your opponent to move laterally while you recover your own position.

## Where It Goes Wrong

If your wrist collapses at that low pressure, the ball nets — you've lost your touch structure entirely. If your grip pressure creeps up to 4 or 5 instead of staying at 2, the ball flies long — you simply haven't absorbed enough. And if your grip visibly softens before impact, your shot gets telegraphed and your opponent reads it and passes you.
