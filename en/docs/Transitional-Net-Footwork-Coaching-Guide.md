# Transitional Net Footwork

Moving from the baseline to the net, executing your approach shot, and hitting volleys under real time pressure all need a specific set of movement patterns — and getting them right is what turns the net from a place you panic into a zone where you actually dominate space.

## How It Works

Moving to net is fundamentally about stealing time from your opponent. It starts with a genuinely good approach shot hit with forward momentum — often from a closed stance. The critical piece: you need to execute a transition split-step as you're moving forward, pausing momentarily right as your opponent strikes their passing shot. Once you're actually at net, your movement should rely on small adjustments and a compact, stable structure — not big, lunging steps.

## Where It Goes Wrong

Running blindly — sprinting to net without splitting — leaves you unable to change direction, and you get passed easily. Swinging at your volleys with huge groundstroke-sized swings breaks your timing and sends balls sailing past the back fence. And dead feet — standing completely still after your first volley — leaves you unable to adjust to whatever comes next, exposed to lobs and sharp angles you should've been able to cover.

## How to Train It

Practice what I'd call the two-volley pattern: hit your approach shot, execute your transition split-step, hit a first volley, make your small adjustments, then hit a finishing volley. The focus throughout is controlled aggression and compact mechanics — not size, not power for its own sake, just clean, repeatable structure under real time pressure.
