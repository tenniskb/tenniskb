# Volley Footwork Sequence

Every volley you'll ever hit runs through the same three-phase footwork sequence: pivot your outside foot, move forward, then step with your inside foot right at contact. What changes is which of three directions that final step takes, depending on where the ball actually arrives.

## Phase 1: Pivot Your Outside Foot

Right after your split-step, your first movement is your outside foot pivoting from perpendicular to nearly parallel with the net — your right foot on a forehand volley, your left on a backhand. This isn't a full step. It's a rotational commitment to a direction, and it does two things at once: it points your knees where you're about to go, and it pre-loads that outside leg for the push-off that follows.

## Phase 2: Move Forward

After the pivot, move toward the ball as aggressively as its speed allows. On a fast ball, you may only have time for the pivot itself — your forward movement gets compressed right into it. On a slower ball, you can take multiple steps forward, and you should use every one of them: getting as close to the net as possible gives your opponent less time to respond, opens sharper angles for you, raises your contact point, and forces your opponent's next passing shot to clear the net from lower down. Every one of those advantages compounds the closer you get.

## Phase 3: Step With Your Inside Foot at Contact

Your final step before contact is your inside foot landing forward into the shot — left foot on a forehand, right on a backhand. The ideal is for that foot to land slightly airborne right at the moment of contact — your weight still moving through the hitting zone as you strike, feeding that "freight train" carry through the ball. If your foot's already firmly planted before contact happens, your momentum has already stopped, and you've created what I'd call a mechanical leak — energy that should've gone into the shot, wasted.

## Three Directions, Three Situations

**The straight step** handles balls comfortably 2-3 feet to your side — left foot stepping toward roughly noon-to-1 o'clock on a forehand, right foot toward noon-to-11 on a backhand. This is your standard volley position, and if time allows, this is exactly where you want your feet: shoulders turned, legs positioned to drive forward into the shot.

**The lunge step** handles wider balls needing a real reach — left foot toward 2-to-3 o'clock on a forehand, right foot toward 9-to-10 on a backhand. This one demands strong legs and real balance, since you need to stay low and keep your center of gravity under control at full extension. Keeping that center of gravity low is what lets you stop quickly and recover for whatever comes next.

**The away step** handles a ball hit fast directly at your body, where you only have time for your front foot to step away from it — left foot toward 9-to-10 o'clock on a forehand, right foot toward 1-to-2 on a backhand. This step creates space: it moves your body clear of the incoming ball, giving your arms room to swing with your shoulders level and your balance intact. Skip it, and you get jammed — the classic chicken-wing compensation, elbow forced out and away with no room to work.

## One More Detail: Keep Your Racket Head Tilted Up

Whenever possible, your racket head should tilt up rather than down. There's a real mechanical reason for this — resisting a push-back on a racket tilted 45 degrees up is measurably easier than on one tilted 45 degrees down, because the skeletal alignment behind an upward-tilted face is doing the work, instead of your muscles straining to hold a downward tilt in place. On a waist-high ball, crouch down and tilt your racket head upward to effectively turn it into a chest-high contact. On a genuinely low ball — knee to ankle height — crouch enough to keep your racket face horizontal rather than angling it down.
